01 Overview

Overview

Artech LLC is a US-headquartered IT staffing and solutions company with active hiring in India. They currently have 162 open Software Engineer roles. As a staffing firm, Artech places engineers at client companies, which means their interviews evaluate two things at once: your technical skills and your readiness to work in a client-facing environment.

The process typically runs 2-3 rounds. Candidates report an initial HR screening call covering background, notice period, and location, followed by one or two technical rounds. Depending on the engagement, the technical round may be led by Artech's team or handed directly to the end client.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

Artech's interviews mix standard engineering questions with checks on availability and client fit. Candidates report the following coming up frequently:

  1. Walk me through a project you owned end to end. What decisions did you make and why?
  2. Which programming languages and frameworks do you work with most? Give a recent example of using them.
  3. How do you handle it when client requirements change after development has already started?
  4. Explain the difference between REST and GraphQL. When would you choose one over the other?
  5. Tell me about a bug you found in production. What was your debugging process?
  6. How do you approach performance issues in a backend service or web application?
  7. Describe a time you disagreed with a stakeholder or client. How did you handle it?
  8. What is your experience with cloud platforms such as AWS, Azure, or GCP? Which services have you used in production?
  9. How do you work in an Agile or Scrum team? What does your typical sprint look like?
  10. Have you worked directly with clients or end users? How did you manage their expectations?
  11. What is your notice period and are you open to working on-site at a client location if needed?
  12. How do you keep your technical skills up to date?

Questions 3, 7, and 10 carry extra weight at staffing firms because managing client relationships is part of the day-to-day job.

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Q: Walk me through a project you owned end to end.

*Situation:* My team was rebuilding an internal inventory tracking tool that was causing daily data sync failures for a retail client.

*Task:* I was the lead developer responsible for redesigning the backend and the API layer.

*Action:* I mapped out the failure points in the existing system, proposed splitting the sync and reporting modules into separate services, set up a staging environment to validate data integrity, and coordinated load testing with the QA engineer before release.

*Result:* Sync failures dropped to near zero after launch and the client reported faster report generation. The project shipped on schedule.

---

Q: How do you handle it when client requirements change mid-project?

*Situation:* Midway through an engagement, the client decided to change the primary data model after two sprints of work were already complete.

*Task:* I needed to assess the impact honestly and keep the project from going off track.

*Action:* I ran a quick impact analysis and documented which modules would need rework. I set up a call with the client to walk them through the trade-offs: reworking immediately versus deferring to a later phase. We agreed to deliver the original sprint scope first, then fold the model change into the next sprint with a revised timeline.

*Result:* We delivered on time and the client signed off on the updated plan without escalation.

---

Q: Describe a time you pushed back on a stakeholder.

*Situation:* A project manager wanted to skip unit testing for a payment integration feature to hit a deadline.

*Task:* I had to make the case for keeping the tests without damaging the working relationship.

*Action:* I referenced two past incidents where untested payment code caused rollbacks and proposed a middle path: write tests only for the critical payment flow and defer edge-case coverage to the next sprint.

*Result:* The PM agreed. We shipped on time, the tests caught one integration bug before release, and the relationship stayed intact.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is the most reliable framework for behavioral questions at Artech. Most 'tell me about a time...' questions map to it directly.

  • *Situation:* Set the scene in one or two sentences.
  • *Task:* State your specific responsibility.
  • *Action:* Focus on what YOU did. Use 'I' not 'we.' This is the longest part of the answer.
  • *Result:* What changed because of your action? Quantify if you can, but only use numbers you can genuinely stand behind.

For technical questions, use a think-aloud approach: state your understanding of the problem, name the trade-offs you see, then explain your choice. Interviewers at staffing firms often care as much about how you communicate your reasoning as whether you reach the correct answer, because you will need to explain technical decisions to non-technical clients on a regular basis.

For 'why Artech' questions, talk about the variety of client exposure and the chance to build skills across different industries and tech stacks. Be specific about what kind of client environments interest you. Generic answers about growth or learning without a concrete angle rarely land well.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Artech interviewers typically evaluate you across three dimensions:

Technical competence. Be fluent in at least one primary language and its ecosystem, comfortable with SQL, and able to discuss system design at a level appropriate to your experience. Entry-level candidates get straightforward coding questions; mid and senior candidates typically face design and trade-off discussions.

Client readiness. This matters more at a staffing firm than at a product company. Can you explain a technical decision to someone non-technical? Can you say 'I do not know, let me check and come back to you' without flinching? Interviewers flag candidates who seem rigid or who cannot adapt when a client pushes back.

Logistics clarity. Staffing firms work to client timelines. Know your exact notice period, whether your current company offers a buyout option, and whether you can work on-site at a client location. Vague answers here slow the process down even after a strong technical round.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Week 1: Technical foundation

Revise data structures, algorithms, and common coding patterns in your primary language (arrays, hashmaps, trees, recursion). Practice writing clean code without an IDE. Revise SQL: joins, aggregations, and indexing. If the role calls for a specific stack such as Java, Python, or React, re-read your own project code and be ready to explain the decisions you made.

Week 2: Behavioural prep

Write out five or six STAR stories from your work history covering: a complex technical challenge, a client or stakeholder conflict, a missed deadline, a time you learned something quickly, and a project you are proud of. Practice saying each one out loud in under three minutes.

Week 3: Client and communication prep

Practice explaining a technical concept in plain language, for example, how does an API work, or what does a cache actually do? Research the industries Artech typically serves from your target city and think about how your background maps to those client environments.

Before the interview day

Confirm your notice period and salary expectation. Have a specific number ready based on the salary bands above. Know your preferred city and your position on travel or on-site client work. These questions come up in the HR round, and hesitation signals that you have not thought it through.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Treating it like a pure product company interview. Artech places engineers at client sites, so communication and client fit are evaluated as seriously as technical ability. Candidates who prepare only for coding questions and stumble on 'how do you handle a difficult client?' often do not progress past the first technical round.

Vague availability answers. 'I can join soon' is not useful to a staffing recruiter. Know your exact notice period and whether a buyout is possible. Artech works to client-driven timelines, and ambiguity here can cost you an offer even after a strong technical round.

Underselling domain experience. If you have worked in fintech, e-commerce, or healthcare IT, say so clearly. Artech matches engineers to client industry needs, and specific domain experience widens your placement options considerably.

Over-explaining on technical questions. Talking for several minutes on a simple question does not always signal depth. It can signal poor communication instincts, which is a red flag in a client-facing role. Answer what was asked, then pause and let the interviewer lead.

Not asking about the end client. If Artech is hiring for a specific client engagement, you are allowed to ask what the client domain is and what the team structure looks like. Asking good questions signals genuine interest and helps you decide whether the role is the right fit.

Saving logistics for the offer stage. Salary expectations, location constraints, and travel flexibility should be settled in the first HR call. Surprises at the offer stage waste time on both sides.
