01 Overview

Overview

Adyen is a global payments company that processes transactions for major merchants worldwide. Their Engineering Manager role sits at the intersection of technical leadership and people management, with a strong emphasis on reliability, speed, and ownership. The interview process is thorough and typically includes a recruiter screen, a conversation with the hiring manager, and several structured rounds that test leadership style, technical judgment, and ability to work across teams.

Candidates report that Adyen interviewers go deep on real examples. They want to see how you have actually handled messy, high-stakes situations, not how you would handle a hypothetical. The company values people who think like owners and who understand the impact of payment downtime on real merchants and customers.

Adyen currently has 253 open roles listed on their careers page. As of July 2026, there are 975 Engineering Manager positions live across India, with Bangalore leading at 182 openings. Salary bands for this level typically run 35-60 LPA for Manager roles and 55-90 LPA at the Senior Manager level.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

Here are 12 questions that candidates report seeing frequently in Adyen Engineering Manager interviews. These reflect Adyen's known focus on ownership, reliability, and people leadership.

  1. Walk me through how you have built or grown an engineering team from the ground up, or significantly scaled one.
  2. How do you balance shipping product features fast with keeping a payments system reliable and secure?
  3. Tell me about a time you disagreed with a product manager or business stakeholder. How did you resolve it?
  4. How do you handle a team member who is consistently missing expectations? Walk me through a real situation.
  5. Adyen operates across many markets and time zones. Tell me about a time you managed a critical production incident that affected live customers.
  6. How do you set the technical direction for your team when priorities keep changing?
  7. Tell me about a cross-functional project you owned. What did you learn from how it went?
  8. How do you keep engineers technically sharp while they are heads-down on delivery?
  9. Describe a time you had to make a major technical decision with limited data. What was your process?
  10. Tell me about a time you introduced a new engineering practice. What resistance did you face and how did you handle it?
  11. How do you decide when to build something in-house versus using a third-party solution?
  12. Give me an example of a time you shipped something critical under a tight deadline without cutting corners on quality.

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Q: Tell me about a time you managed a critical production incident that affected live customers.

*Situation:* Our payment processing service went down during a high-traffic sale event for one of our top clients. Transactions were failing for several minutes before our alerting caught it.

*Task:* As the Engineering Manager on call, I had to coordinate a fix, communicate with stakeholders, and keep my team focused under pressure.

*Action:* I immediately pulled in the two engineers with the deepest context on that service and set up a war-room call. I delegated technical triage to them while I handled communication, sending status updates every few minutes to the client success team and our VP of Engineering. Once we identified the root cause (a config change pushed shortly before the outage), we rolled it back and verified recovery with live transaction checks.

*Result:* Service was restored within the hour. We ran a blameless post-mortem the next day and added automated config validation to our deploy pipeline. The client received a full incident report by the following morning and stayed with us long term.

---

Q: Tell me about a time you disagreed with a business stakeholder on a technical decision.

*Situation:* A product director wanted us to ship a new checkout flow in two weeks to meet a marketing deadline. My team had flagged that the database schema changes needed were risky without proper migration testing.

*Task:* I needed to push back while keeping the business relationship intact and finding a path forward.

*Action:* I scheduled a focused working session with the product director and brought our tech lead. I walked through the specific risk: if the migration failed mid-deploy, we would have partial data corruption affecting real transactions. I proposed an alternative, which was to ship a feature-flagged version on the original deadline that was safe to roll back, then fully enable it after proper testing the following week.

*Result:* The director agreed once they understood the financial risk to the business. We shipped on the original deadline behind a feature flag, enabled it fully one week later with zero issues, and the director became a stronger advocate for technical risk conversations going forward.

---

Q: Tell me about a time you introduced a new engineering practice that improved your team.

*Situation:* My team had no consistent code review process. Reviews were sporadic, often skipped under deadline pressure, and we were seeing recurring bugs in production.

*Task:* I needed to build a review culture without slowing down delivery or making the team feel policed.

*Action:* I started by running a short retrospective where the team themselves identified code quality as a pain point. I proposed a lightweight checklist and a rule that every pull request needed at least one review before merge. I also did the first round of reviews myself to model the behavior and show it did not have to be slow. I paired this with a weekly session where we traced recent production issues back to their origin.

*Result:* Within two months the team had internalized the practice. Production bugs dropped noticeably, and in our next retrospective engineers said they felt more confident in the code they were shipping.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is the core structure Adyen interviewers expect. Keep the Situation brief (two to three sentences), make the Action the longest part covering what you specifically did rather than what the team did, and quantify the Result where you honestly can.

For technical judgment questions, use a simple decision log format: state what options you considered, what data or constraints shaped your thinking, what you chose, and what you would do differently today. This shows you are reflective and data-aware, which Adyen values strongly.

For cross-functional or stakeholder questions, lead with the business context first. Interviewers at Adyen want to see that you understand why something mattered to the company, not just how you resolved a technical problem.

For people leadership questions, be specific about the individual. Avoid vague language like 'I supported them' and instead name the exact conversation, the exact feedback, and the exact outcome. Adyen interviewers probe for depth here and will follow up with 'what exactly did you say?' type questions.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Ownership without hand-holding. Adyen operates with relatively flat structures and expects managers to make decisions without waiting for permission. Show examples where you took initiative on hard calls, especially ones where the outcome was uncertain.

Technical credibility. You do not need to write code in the interview, but you should be able to discuss system design trade-offs, explain past architectural decisions, and push back on bad technical ideas with sound reasoning.

Speed paired with quality. Payments infrastructure cannot afford shortcuts, but it also cannot move slowly. Interviewers look for evidence that you have internalized this tension and have practical ways of navigating it. Generic answers about 'moving fast and breaking things' will hurt you here.

Clear, direct communication. Adyen values people who say what they think. Candidates report that interviewers appreciate concise, honest answers more than polished corporate language.

Data-informed thinking. Even on people decisions, show that you use data: ticket velocity, incident frequency, team health surveys, or patterns from your 1:1s. This signals you are not just going by gut feel.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Step 1: Study Adyen's public engineering blog and their stated values. They publish regularly about their payment architecture, reliability standards, and how they think about scale. This gives you real vocabulary and context to use in the interview.

Step 2: Write down 8 to 10 stories from your own career that cover incident management, stakeholder conflict, team building, technical decisions under uncertainty, and process improvements. Practice telling each one in the STAR format in under three minutes.

Step 3: Prepare your 'why Adyen' answer. Be specific. Mention their merchant-first model, their platform approach to payments, or a specific engineering challenge they have written about. Generic answers about 'scale and innovation' will not land.

Step 4: Do a live practice run. Ask a peer or mentor to interview you using the questions in this guide. Record yourself and review how concise and specific your answers actually are.

Step 5: Prepare smart questions to ask. Adyen interviewers typically leave time for your questions. Ask about how Engineering Managers are measured, how cross-team dependencies are handled, or what the on-call culture looks like for managers.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Being too vague. The single most common failure candidates report is giving answers that sound good but contain no specific detail. 'I helped the team improve' is not an answer. 'I ran weekly 1:1s where I tracked specific growth goals per engineer and reviewed them monthly' is an answer.

Over-claiming team results. Interviewers will probe whether you personally drove an outcome or whether the team did. Use 'I' for decisions you owned and 'we' for shared work. Be honest about the distinction, because follow-up questions will expose any inflation.

Ignoring the fintech context. Adyen is not a generic software company. If you talk about shipping fast without acknowledging reliability and financial risk, you will seem like a poor fit. Weave payment-system sensitivity into your answers naturally.

Rambling. Adyen interviews are structured and time-bound. If you spend too long on one question, you will run out of time and signal poor communication habits. Practice getting to the point quickly and then pausing to let the interviewer follow up.

Not knowing your own numbers. If you claim you reduced incidents, know roughly by how much. If you grew a team, know from what size to what size. Saying you do not remember the exact figure once is fine. Doing it repeatedly signals low ownership of your outcomes.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

How many rounds does the Adyen Engineering Manager interview typically have?

Candidates report a process that typically includes a recruiter phone screen, a hiring manager conversation, and two to four structured interview rounds. The rounds generally cover leadership, technical judgment, and cross-functional collaboration. Adyen may also include a final conversation with a senior engineering leader. The exact structure can vary by team, so ask your recruiter for the specific plan for your role.

Does Adyen ask coding questions for Engineering Manager roles?

Most candidates report that Adyen EM interviews do not include live coding. The focus is on technical judgment, system design discussions, and leadership scenarios. However, you should be prepared to walk through past architectural decisions and explain trade-offs clearly. Being technically articulate matters even if you are not writing code on a whiteboard.

How important is fintech or payments experience for this role?

Adyen values candidates who understand reliability, compliance, and the real consequences of payment failures. Candidates from non-fintech backgrounds have successfully joined by showing they grasp the stakes of high-availability systems. Focus on examples where quality, uptime, and financial impact were real concerns in your past work. Research Adyen's specific technical challenges and merchant use cases before the interview to show genuine context.

What salary can I expect for an Engineering Manager role at Adyen India?

Based on knok's jobradar data, Engineering Manager roles in India typically range from 35-60 LPA, with Senior Manager roles going up to 55-90 LPA. Actual compensation depends on your level, years of experience, and the specific team you join. For the most current figures broken down by company and city, check publicly reported data on Glassdoor or levels.fyi.

How should I prepare for cross-functional leadership questions?

Pick a real example where you had to align two or more teams that had different priorities or timelines. Be specific about who the stakeholders were, what the conflict was, and what you personally did to resolve it. Adyen is particularly interested in how you influence without authority, so examples where you had no formal power over the other team tend to land better. Practice telling the story in under three minutes using the STAR format.

Is there a take-home assignment in the Adyen Engineering Manager interview?

Candidates typically do not report a take-home assignment for Engineering Manager roles. The rounds are usually conversation-based. However, some teams may share a case study or a system design prompt to discuss live during the interview. Confirm with your recruiter whether any written exercise is part of your specific process, as this can vary by team and hiring manager.
