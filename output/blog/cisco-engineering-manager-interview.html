01 Overview

Overview

Cisco is one of the world's largest networking, cybersecurity, and collaboration companies, and its Engineering Manager (EM) role is central to how the company ships products. As of July 2026, Cisco has 208 open Engineering Manager and adjacent roles tracked by knok's job radar, making it one of the most active hirers in this space right now.

The interview process typically spans multiple rounds covering four broad areas: people leadership, technical depth, cross-functional collaboration, and culture fit with Cisco's values. Candidates report that rounds include a recruiter screen, a hiring manager conversation, a panel with senior engineers and cross-functional partners, and sometimes a presentation or case-study exercise. Expect the full process to last a few weeks.

For context on the broader market, knok is tracking 975 Engineering Manager openings across India as of July 2026. Bangalore leads with 182 openings, followed by Delhi (53), Pune (20), Chennai (20), Hyderabad (16), and Mumbai (12).

Salary bands based on knok data: Manager roles fall in the 35-60 LPA range, Senior Manager in 55-90 LPA, and Director-level in the 90-150+ LPA range. For Cisco-specific numbers, Glassdoor and levels.fyi have publicly reported data from current and former employees in India.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

These are the questions Cisco EM candidates most commonly report encountering, based on publicly available interview feedback.

  1. Cisco talks about 'Conscious Culture' and inclusion. How have you actively built a diverse and inclusive team?
  2. Tell me about a time you had to manage a consistently underperforming engineer. What did you do and what was the outcome?
  3. How do you balance paying down technical debt against delivering new features on a tight roadmap?
  4. Describe a situation where your team disagreed with a decision made by leadership above you. How did you handle it?
  5. Cisco works across networking hardware, software, and cloud. How do you manage engineers across very different technical domains?
  6. Give an example of a time you scaled your team rapidly. How did you maintain quality and culture during that growth?
  7. How do you measure the health and productivity of your engineering team?
  8. Describe how you drove alignment between engineering, product, and a go-to-market team on a shared goal.
  9. Cisco is customer-obsessed. Tell me about a decision you changed specifically because of customer feedback.
  10. How do you approach retaining top engineers who have competing offers from other companies?
  11. Tell me about a major technical incident your team faced. How did you lead them through it?
  12. How do you keep a team motivated and focused during long, ambiguous, or multi-year projects?

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Q: Tell me about a time you managed a consistently underperforming engineer.

*Situation:* A senior engineer on my team who had been strong previously started missing deadlines and producing code with frequent issues after a team restructuring.

*Task:* My job was to address the performance gap clearly and fairly, without letting it drag down the rest of the team.

*Action:* I set up a private 1-on-1 to understand what had changed. The engineer felt disconnected from the new product direction. I worked with them to find a sub-project matching their strengths, set clear written goals with two-week checkpoints, and arranged peer pairing with a senior colleague. I was direct that the current trajectory was not sustainable and documented each conversation.

*Result:* Output improved noticeably within a few weeks, and the engineer became a key contributor to a feature release. I also built a quarterly 'alignment check' into my team rhythm to catch similar drift earlier.

---

Q: How did you drive alignment between engineering, product, and a go-to-market team on a shared goal?

*Situation:* We were launching a new platform feature where engineering, product, and the sales team had conflicting priorities, which was causing delays and constant rework.

*Task:* I needed to get three teams with different incentives to agree on a shared definition of 'done' before we wasted more cycles.

*Action:* I proposed a structured alignment session where each team listed their top must-haves and one nice-to-have. I facilitated a trade-off conversation to find the overlap, documented the agreed scope in a one-pager that all three leads signed off on, and set up a weekly cross-functional sync for the project duration.

*Result:* The launch happened on schedule. The sales team confirmed the final feature matched what they had promised customers, and we cut scope-related rework significantly compared to the previous launch cycle.

---

Q: Tell me about a major technical incident your team faced and how you led them through it.

*Situation:* A production outage took down a core API that many downstream teams depended on, right before a major customer demo.

*Task:* I had to lead the incident response, keep stakeholders informed, and make sure the team did not burn out under pressure.

*Action:* I set up a dedicated incident channel immediately and assigned clear roles: one engineer on investigation, one on internal communications, one on customer-facing updates. I kept the response focused on restoration first and root cause second. I updated senior leadership every thirty minutes with a short, plain-language status note so there were no surprises.

*Result:* The API was restored in time for the demo. The post-mortem surfaced a monitoring gap that the team fixed in the next sprint. Leadership specifically noted that communication during the incident was unusually clear and calm.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

STAR for behavioral questions is the expected format at Cisco. Every answer needs a clear Situation, Task, Action, and Result. Keep Situation and Task tight (one to two sentences each), spend most of your time on Action, and always close with a concrete Result. Trailing off without a result is one of the most common reasons candidates do not advance.

The 'People, Process, Product' lens works well for leadership questions. When describing how you run your team, cover all three threads: how you grow the people, how you set up the process so the team moves fast, and how you ensure the product outcome is right. Cisco interviewers typically listen for all three.

The 'Disagree and Commit' framing is useful for conflict-with-leadership questions. Show that you can push back respectfully with data, but once a decision is made, you fully commit and carry the message to your team without undermining leadership. Cisco values both the pushback and the follow-through.

For technical credibility questions, frame answers around how you make good technical decisions with your team, not how you make them alone. Cisco EMs are expected to be technical enough to earn engineers' respect, not necessarily the best individual coder on the team.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Cisco EM interviewers typically look for a few specific signals, based on what candidates publicly report.

Genuine ownership. Cisco wants managers who treat their team's outcomes as their own. Answers that lean on 'they' instead of 'I', or that blame external factors without showing what you did anyway, tend to score lower.

Cultural awareness. Cisco has invested heavily in its 'Conscious Culture' program around inclusion, trust, and psychological safety. Interviewers probe whether you have genuinely thought about these topics or are just reciting talking points. Expect follow-up questions if your answer sounds rehearsed.

Technical credibility. You do not need to write production code daily, but you do need to talk fluently about architecture decisions, trade-offs, and how you evaluate technical quality in your team. Candidates who cannot engage at this level often struggle.

Scale and complexity. Cisco is a large, globally distributed company. They want evidence that you have managed across time zones, navigated organisational complexity, or thought carefully about how processes break down at scale.

Customer focus. Cisco's products serve large enterprises. Interviewers check whether you connect your team's work to customer outcomes, not just internal velocity metrics.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Week 1: Research and grounding. Read Cisco's recent press releases and investor materials to understand current priorities such as AI networking, cybersecurity, and cloud. Study the job description carefully and map each requirement to a specific experience you have. Look up the hiring team on LinkedIn to understand what products and challenges they own.

Week 2: Story building. Write out six to eight STAR stories covering the main themes: performance management, cross-functional alignment, incident response, team scaling, and customer focus. Practice saying each one out loud, aiming for two to three minutes per story. Record yourself once to catch filler words and vague language.

Week 3: Technical and culture prep. Review engineering leadership topics relevant to Cisco's work, such as distributed systems, DevOps practices, and CI/CD pipelines. Read about Cisco's Conscious Culture on their public careers pages. Prepare two to three specific questions for each interviewer, focused on real team challenges rather than general company facts.

Before each round: Review the STAR stories that best match that interviewer's background. Prepare a one-sentence headline for each story so you can pivot quickly if the conversation changes direction.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Being too vague. Cisco interviewers push hard for specifics. Saying 'I improved team morale' without explaining what you actually did and what changed is the most common reason candidates do not advance.

Skipping the result. Many candidates give strong Situation and Action answers but trail off without a clear outcome. Always close the loop: what happened, what did you measure, and what did you learn?

Overclaiming technical depth. Cisco EMs need technical credibility, not superhero status. Claiming you personally wrote all the critical code often raises doubts about your ability to delegate and trust your team.

Dismissing culture questions. Some candidates treat Cisco's Conscious Culture questions as soft filler and give generic answers. These questions are scored the same as technical and behavioural ones and interviewers do push back on vague replies.

Weak closing questions. Cisco interviewers note whether a candidate's questions show genuine curiosity about the team and its real challenges. Asking only about compensation or benefits at this stage is a common misstep.

Preparing only for behavioural questions. Cisco EM rounds frequently include a discussion of a real technical or organisational scenario. Candidates who have only rehearsed STAR stories can get caught off guard when the conversation shifts to systems design or process trade-offs.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

How many rounds does the Cisco EM interview typically have?

Candidates report that the Cisco EM process typically includes three to five rounds. This usually covers a recruiter screen, a hiring manager conversation, and a panel with engineers and cross-functional partners. Some roles also include a presentation or case-study round, particularly at the Senior Manager or Director level. The number of rounds varies by team and seniority.

Does Cisco ask coding questions in EM interviews?

Typically, Cisco does not ask EM candidates to write code on a whiteboard or solve algorithmic problems. Candidates report that system design discussions and architecture trade-off questions are common instead. Being able to talk fluently about technical decisions and their consequences is expected. Writing code yourself is not.

What is Cisco's Conscious Culture and why does it come up so often in interviews?

Cisco's Conscious Culture is a company-wide program focused on inclusion, trust, and psychological safety within teams. It shows up in interviews because Cisco actively scores leadership candidates on how these values appear in their actual management decisions, not just in stated beliefs. Prepare real examples of times you created a more inclusive or psychologically safe environment for your team, and expect follow-up questions if your answer sounds generic.

How long does the Cisco EM hiring process take end to end?

Candidates report the process typically takes a few weeks from the first recruiter screen to a final decision. The timeline can stretch if there are multiple approval layers or if the role is at a more senior level. Following up politely with your recruiter after each round is standard practice and usually welcomed.

What salary can I expect as an Engineering Manager at Cisco in India?

Based on knok data, Manager-level roles in this space fall in the 35-60 LPA range, Senior Manager roles in the 55-90 LPA range, and Director-level roles in the 90-150+ LPA range. For Cisco-specific figures, Glassdoor and levels.fyi have publicly reported data from current and former Cisco employees in India. The final number depends on your level, the specific team, and how you negotiate.

How should I prepare if I am coming from a startup and have not managed at Cisco's scale?

Focus on the principles behind your decisions rather than the absolute size of your team or budget. Cisco interviewers typically care about whether you can think clearly about scale, cross-functional complexity, and structured process, even if you have not managed hundreds of engineers. Be honest about the differences and show you have thought concretely about how you would adapt your approach inside a large, globally distributed organisation.
