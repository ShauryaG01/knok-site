01 Hiring Overview

Hiring Overview

GitLab is a fully remote, globally distributed company that builds the leading DevSecOps platform used by millions of developers worldwide. Unlike most tech firms, GitLab has no physical offices. Every team member works remotely, including a growing number of professionals based in India.

As of early July 2026, GitLab has 170 open roles across engineering, product, sales, marketing, and customer success. Indian professionals can apply to many of these positions, since GitLab hires across time zones and supports remote work from India for most roles.

Getting hired at GitLab is competitive but highly transparent. The company publishes its entire hiring process in a public handbook, so you always know what to expect at each stage. If you prefer a structured, documentation-driven work culture over office politics, GitLab can be a strong career move.

03 Interview Process

Interview Process

GitLab's hiring process typically runs across five to six stages. The exact stages vary by role and level, but here is what most candidates go through.

Application and screening. Submit your resume and a short cover letter through GitLab's careers page. The recruiting team reviews applications and shortlists candidates who match the role requirements. Response times vary; some candidates hear back within a few days, while others wait a couple of weeks.

Recruiter call. A GitLab recruiter reaches out for a short video call covering your background, interest in the company, compensation expectations, and logistics questions. Be ready to explain why you want to work at an all-remote company, since GitLab takes culture fit seriously from the very first conversation.

Hiring manager interview. This is a deeper conversation with the person who would be your direct manager. Expect questions about your past work, how you approach complex problems, and how you communicate in an async environment. Prepare specific examples from your career using the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result).

Technical or role-specific interviews. For engineering roles, this stage typically includes a coding exercise or a system design discussion. For non-engineering roles, you may be given a case study, a writing task, or a take-home assignment. GitLab commonly uses real-world scenarios rather than abstract puzzles, which most candidates find more fair.

Values interview. GitLab runs a dedicated values-fit interview, usually with someone outside your immediate team. This focuses on whether you genuinely align with GitLab's CREDIT values: Collaboration, Results, Efficiency, Diversity, Iteration, Transparency. Authentic and specific answers matter more than rehearsed ones.

Reference checks and offer. GitLab contacts references before extending an offer. The offer includes base compensation, equity, and details about GitLab's remote work stipend. You can negotiate at this stage.

04 What They Look For

What They Look For

GitLab looks for people who can thrive in an all-remote, async-first environment. A few qualities stand out across all roles.

Async communication skills. GitLab does almost everything in writing, through issues, merge requests, and shared documents rather than meetings. If you can write clearly, structure your thoughts, and keep teammates informed without relying on real-time calls, you are already ahead of most candidates.

Self-management and ownership. There is no manager walking past your desk at GitLab. The company hires people who set their own priorities, meet commitments without being chased, and flag problems early rather than waiting for someone to ask.

Technical depth in your domain. Whether you are an engineer, a marketer, or a finance professional, GitLab expects you to know your craft deeply. For engineering roles, solid fundamentals in your stack matter more than familiarity with GitLab's specific product.

Alignment with CREDIT values. GitLab's six values are embedded into performance reviews, peer feedback, and hiring decisions. Interviewers actively probe for Iteration (shipping small, learning fast) and Transparency (sharing context openly, even when it is uncomfortable).

Remote work readiness. You do not need to have worked fully remote before, but you should understand what it requires. Candidates who have managed their own schedule, worked across time zones, or contributed to open-source projects find it easier to demonstrate this fit.

05 How To Prepare

How To Prepare

Preparing for GitLab interviews is different from preparing for most Indian IT company interviews. Here is what actually moves the needle.

Read the GitLab handbook. GitLab publishes its entire internal handbook publicly. Reading sections on values, engineering practices, and hiring gives you genuine insight rather than generic talking points. Referencing specific handbook details during your interviews signals that you have done the real work.

Practice writing before you interview. GitLab often asks candidates to complete written exercises or case studies. Before your interviews, practice writing clear and concise documents: summaries, proposals, and technical explanations. The goal is to communicate precisely with no back-and-forth needed.

Prepare STAR examples aligned to CREDIT. Map strong examples from your career to each of the CREDIT values. Focus especially on Iteration (a time you shipped something small and improved it quickly) and Results (a measurable outcome you owned end-to-end). Avoid vague stories; specifics win every time.

Know the product. Create a free GitLab account and use it. Understand what CI/CD pipelines do, how merge requests work, and what pain points developers face. Even for non-engineering roles, product familiarity shows genuine interest in the company's mission.

06 Culture And Values

Culture And Values

GitLab is one of the largest and most well-documented all-remote companies in the world. For Indian professionals used to office-based IT work, the culture can feel very different, and for many people, it is a welcome change.

Async-first by design. Most communication at GitLab happens through written issues, merge requests, and documents rather than meetings. Your work output and written communication matter far more than being online at a specific hour. This works well for Indian professionals collaborating across multiple time zones.

Radical transparency. GitLab makes an unusual amount of information public, from its handbook to its company strategy to salary bands by region. Inside the company, this transparency extends to how decisions are made and communicated. People are expected to share context freely rather than hoarding it.

Iteration over perfection. GitLab's engineering and product culture strongly favors shipping something small and improving it, over planning a perfect solution for months. If you are used to long release cycles or waterfall processes, this mindset shift takes deliberate practice.

CREDIT values in practice. Every team member is evaluated partly on how well they live the CREDIT values: Collaboration, Results, Efficiency, Diversity, Iteration, Transparency. These show up in performance reviews, peer feedback, and everyday decisions across the company.

India-specific context. GitLab hires in India and benchmarks compensation to local market rates using a publicly disclosed formula. Glassdoor lists commonly cited ranges for various roles and levels. Working hours are generally flexible, though some overlap with US time zones may be expected depending on your team.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

Can Indian professionals apply to GitLab's open roles?

Yes, GitLab hires in India and posts roles that are open to Indian candidates or to remote applicants worldwide. As of early July 2026, GitLab has 170 open roles globally, and a portion of these are India-eligible. Check the careers page and filter by location to find roles that list India or 'remote' as an option.

What salary can I expect at GitLab in India?

GitLab benchmarks compensation to local market rates using a publicly disclosed formula tied to your role, level, and location. Glassdoor and levels.fyi list commonly cited ranges for various functions, which vary widely by seniority. GitLab also publishes its compensation calculator publicly, so you can estimate your expected band before you even apply.

How long does the full hiring process take?

Publicly reported timelines suggest the end-to-end process at GitLab commonly runs four to eight weeks from application to offer. Some candidates report faster movement for urgent roles, while senior positions can take longer. The recruiter will usually share an estimated timeline during your first call.

Do I need DevOps or DevSecOps experience to join GitLab?

Not necessarily. GitLab hires across many functions including sales, marketing, finance, legal, and customer success, not just engineering. For engineering roles, familiarity with CI/CD concepts is helpful but not always a hard requirement depending on the team. What matters most is deep expertise in your own domain and genuine interest in the product.

What is the interview process like for engineering roles specifically?

Engineering candidates typically go through a coding or take-home exercise, followed by one or two technical interviews covering their core stack, system design, or both. GitLab avoids abstract brain-teaser puzzles in most cases and prefers problems that reflect real-world scenarios. Publicly reported feedback from candidates suggests the process is rigorous but well-structured and fair.

Does GitLab sponsor visas for Indian candidates?

GitLab's visa sponsorship policies vary by country and role. Since GitLab is remote-first, many Indian professionals work for GitLab from India without needing any visa at all. If you are interested in relocating to another country, check the specific role's details and ask the recruiter directly during your first call.
