01 Overview

Overview

Brex is a US-based fintech company building corporate cards, expense management, and financial software for startups and growing businesses. In 2026, Brex has 279 open roles, signalling continued engineering investment.

The Engineering Manager role at Brex means leading teams that build products handling real business finances at scale. Candidates report a process that typically includes a recruiter screen, a hiring manager conversation, behavioural and leadership rounds, a system design or technical discussion, and a final panel with cross-functional stakeholders.

Brex values ownership culture, directness, and the ability to lead distributed, cross-functional teams. Engineering leaders here are expected to drive technical strategy, not just manage people. If you are targeting this role, strong preparation around fintech reliability, rapid team scaling, and cross-team influence will matter most.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

These are the questions candidates most commonly report from Brex Engineering Manager interviews. They reflect Brex's focus on fintech reliability, ownership, distributed teams, and business alignment.

  1. Brex products handle real money for businesses. How do you build a culture of reliability and zero-defect thinking in your team?
  2. Tell us about a time you scaled an engineering team quickly. How did you approach hiring, onboarding, and keeping quality high?
  3. Brex teams are distributed across time zones. How do you keep a remote or hybrid team executing well without micromanaging?
  4. Describe a situation where you had to re-prioritise your roadmap because of a sudden business or strategic shift.
  5. How do you balance shipping features fast with managing technical debt, especially in a fintech environment where shortcuts can cause serious issues?
  6. Walk us through a critical production incident your team owned. What was your personal role, and what changed afterwards?
  7. Tell us about a time you influenced a product or design decision as an engineering leader, without direct authority over the outcome.
  8. How do you handle an engineer who is technically strong but consistently misses on ownership, delivery, or collaboration?
  9. How do you connect your team's quarterly engineering goals to the company's broader business objectives?
  10. Brex has shifted its product focus more than once. How do you lead your team through a major strategic pivot while maintaining morale and momentum?
  11. How do you evaluate and grow engineers who want to move into technical leadership?
  12. Tell us about a time you had to push back on a product or business ask because the technical risk was too high.

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Q: Walk us through a critical production incident your team owned. What was your personal role and what changed afterwards?

*Situation:* Our payments processing service went down for an extended period on a weekday morning, affecting a large number of transactions and triggering customer escalations.

*Task:* As EM, I needed to coordinate the incident response, communicate clearly to leadership and customers, and make sure we did not repeat this.

*Action:* I jumped into the incident channel immediately, helped triage ownership so the right engineers were on the problem, and sent clear internal status updates every few minutes. Once the service recovered, I facilitated a blameless post-mortem within two days. We identified three root causes: a missing circuit breaker, a manual deployment step with no rollback plan, and an alert that fired too late. I then prioritised fixes for all three in the next sprint and added reliability metrics to our team's quarterly goals.

*Result:* We shipped all three fixes within two weeks. The team reported in retrospectives that they felt more confident handling incidents. We had zero P0 incidents in the following two quarters.

---

Q: How do you handle an engineer who is technically strong but consistently missing on ownership?

*Situation:* One of my senior engineers wrote excellent code but would routinely hand off work without seeing it through to production or customer impact. Other team members started picking up the slack.

*Task:* I needed to address this directly without losing a technically valuable person, and reset expectations around what ownership means at our level.

*Action:* I had a direct one-on-one where I named specific examples rather than giving vague feedback. I asked what was getting in the way and learned the engineer was unclear on where their responsibility ended. Together we wrote a clear definition of 'done' for their role, including monitoring, documentation, and stakeholder sign-off. I also paired them with a project where they had full visibility from kickoff to post-launch review.

*Result:* Within one quarter, their peers noticed the improvement. The engineer told me that clarity on expectations was what they had been missing. They were promoted to Staff the following cycle.

---

Q: Describe a time you had to re-prioritise your roadmap because of a sudden business shift.

*Situation:* Two weeks before a major feature launch, the business decided to sunset one of the product lines our feature was built on top of.

*Task:* I needed to quickly assess what could be salvaged, communicate honestly with my team, and redirect effort without burning people out.

*Action:* I got time with the product lead and the CTO to understand the decision and its timeline. I then mapped every in-flight workstream to three buckets: ship anyway, pause and revisit, and cancel. I shared this map with my team in a candid all-hands, explained the business reasoning, and let people ask hard questions. For cancelled work, I made sure engineers got public credit for what they had built.

*Result:* We redirected the majority of our capacity to a new priority area within one week. Team morale held up better than I expected, and two engineers told me the transparency made them more trusting of leadership.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

Use STAR for most behavioural questions. Situation, Task, Action, Result. Keep Situation short (two sentences), make Action the longest part covering what you personally did, and always quantify the Result where you can.

Use the Influence Map for questions about leading without authority. Sketch who had a stake in the decision, what their concerns were, and how you addressed each one before the final call. Brex interviewers want to see that you understand stakeholder dynamics, not just technical ones.

Use the Reliability Ladder for production and quality questions. Move through: detect fast, contain fast, recover fast, learn permanently. This maps directly to on-call culture in fintech companies like Brex.

Use the Prioritisation Triangle for roadmap questions. Show you weigh three things: business impact, technical risk, and team capacity. Avoid saying you 'just followed product's lead.' Interviewers at Brex want to see EMs with a clear point of view on prioritisation.

Use the Growth Arc for people questions. Describe where the engineer started, what specific coaching or opportunity you gave them, and where they ended up. Concrete before-and-after stories land better than general management philosophy.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Ownership over management. Brex's engineering culture prizes people who run at problems rather than waiting for direction. Interviewers will probe whether you see yourself as an owner of outcomes, not just a process manager.

Fintech instincts. Even if you have not worked in fintech before, demonstrate that you understand what it means to build systems that handle real money. Talk about reliability, audit trails, rollback strategies, and customer trust.

Clarity under pressure. Brex has gone through strategic shifts publicly. Interviewers want to see how you communicate and make decisions when the ground moves. Vague answers about 'staying agile' will not land.

Cross-functional credibility. EM roles here involve working closely with product, design, finance, and compliance. Show that you can build trust across functions, push back constructively, and still deliver.

Data-driven thinking. Bring numbers into your answers wherever possible, covering things like team size, incident frequency, cycle time, and retention. Brex interviewers typically respond well to candidates who can discuss engineering health in measurable terms.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Week 1: Know the company.
Read Brex's engineering blog and any publicly available product announcements from 2024-2026. Understand the core products: corporate cards, expense management, bill pay, and their focus on startup and mid-market customers. Note any reported strategic pivots or product line changes.

Week 2: Build your story bank.
Write out 8-10 experiences using STAR format, covering production incidents, hiring and onboarding, re-prioritisation, performance management, cross-functional influence, and technical debt decisions. Brex will typically hit at least five of these themes.

Week 3: Practice out loud.
Record yourself answering three questions a day. Review for filler words, vagueness on results, and over-long Situation sections. Ask a peer or mentor to play interviewer for one full mock round.

Week 4: Sharpen your questions.
Prepare five or six thoughtful questions to ask Brex interviewers. Good ones include: how engineering and product co-own the roadmap, how on-call and reliability practices are structured, and what a strong first three months looks like for an EM.

On the day: Have your STAR stories in a short cheat sheet. Join or arrive a few minutes early. When you get a broad question, take a moment to pick your best story before you start speaking.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Being vague about your personal role. Saying 'we did X' when the interviewer wants to know what you did. Use 'I' deliberately throughout your answers.

Skipping the result. Many candidates tell great stories but trail off before stating the outcome. Always land the result, even if it was partial or mixed.

Over-rotating on technical depth. Brex EMs are expected to be technically credible, but interviewers are not looking for a senior individual contributor. Balance technical detail with people and business reasoning.

Generic answers about culture. Saying you 'value transparency' or 'foster psychological safety' without a concrete story. Back every principle with an example.

Not asking questions. Candidates who ask no questions typically signal low interest. Prepare genuine questions about the team, the product, and what success looks like in the role.

Ignoring the fintech context. Answers that work for a SaaS or e-commerce EM role may not address Brex's priorities around compliance, reliability, and financial accuracy. Tailor your examples to show you understand what is at stake when software handles real money.

Frequently asked

How many interview rounds does the Brex Engineering Manager process typically have?

Candidates report a process that typically runs four to five rounds: a recruiter screen, a hiring manager conversation, one or two behavioural and leadership rounds, and a system design or technical discussion. Some candidates also report a final panel with cross-functional stakeholders. The exact structure can vary by team, so ask your recruiter at the start what to expect.

Does Brex hire Engineering Managers in India?

Job listings as of July 2026 show active Engineering Manager openings in India, with Bangalore and Delhi among the cities with the most concentration. Brex currently has 279 open roles globally. Availability shifts frequently, so check Brex's careers page directly for the most current listings.

What salary range can an Engineering Manager expect at Brex in India?

Based on 2026 market data, Engineering Manager roles in India typically fall in the 35-60 LPA range, Senior Manager roles in the 55-90 LPA range, and Director-level roles at 90-150 LPA or above. Actual offers depend on years of experience, team scope, and negotiation. For additional data points, check Glassdoor and levels.fyi for Brex-specific compensation reports.

Is there a coding round for Engineering Manager interviews at Brex?

Candidates typically report that live coding tests are not a core part of the EM process at Brex. However, a system design discussion is common, and interviewers may ask technical questions to check whether you can have credible conversations with your engineers. You should be comfortable discussing architecture trade-offs, reliability patterns, and technology choices at a conceptual level.

How long does the full Brex Engineering Manager hiring process take?

Candidates typically report the process spanning three to six weeks from first recruiter contact to offer, though this varies by team and hiring urgency. Delays can happen during background checks or headcount approvals. If you have not heard back after a round, following up once after five to seven business days is standard practice and generally well received.

What should I research about Brex before the interview?

Focus on three areas: Brex's core products (corporate cards, expense management, bill pay), their reported strategic decisions and product focus changes between 2024-2026, and their publicly available engineering content. Understanding why Brex exists, who their customers are, and the compliance environment they operate in will help you frame every answer in terms of real business and financial impact.
