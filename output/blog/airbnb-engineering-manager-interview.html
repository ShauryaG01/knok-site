01 Overview

Overview

Airbnb currently has 242 open Engineering Manager roles, making it one of the more active hirers for this position in 2026. The interview process is known for being values-driven and behavioural, with interviewers probing leadership depth rather than just delivery track records. Candidates report a multi-stage process that typically includes a recruiter screen, a hiring manager conversation, and panel rounds covering people leadership, cross-functional collaboration, and technical judgement.

Salary data from knok jobradar shows bands of 35-60 LPA at the Manager level, 55-90 LPA at Senior Manager, and 90-150+ LPA at Director level. Concrete stories beat polished-sounding principles every time here.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

These questions come up most often in Airbnb EM interviews, based on what candidates report:

  1. Tell me about a time you had to deliver hard feedback to a senior engineer. How did you approach it?
  2. How do you build trust with a new team when you join as their manager?
  3. Describe a situation where your team missed a deadline. What happened and what did you learn?
  4. How do you balance technical debt against product velocity?
  5. Tell me about a time you advocated for your team against a business decision you disagreed with.
  6. How do you identify and develop high-potential engineers on your team?
  7. Describe a cross-functional conflict you navigated. What was your role in resolving it?
  8. How do you approach roadmap planning when stakeholder priorities conflict?
  9. Tell me about a time you had to restructure or rebalance your team. How did you manage the people side of it?
  10. How do you keep engineers motivated during a long, unglamorous infrastructure project?
  11. Describe a time you hired someone who did not work out. What did you do and what would you change?
  12. How do you think about engineering culture, and what have you done to shape it at your current company?

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Q: Tell me about a time you had to deliver hard feedback to a senior engineer.

*Situation:* A senior engineer on my team was technically excellent but consistently talked over junior colleagues in design reviews, which was starting to affect team morale.

*Task:* I needed to address this without damaging our relationship or causing the engineer to disengage.

*Action:* I set up a one-on-one and led with genuine appreciation for their technical contributions. I then shared specific observations rather than generalisations, describing incidents where junior team members went quiet after being interrupted. I asked for their perspective first, which surfaced that they were unaware of the pattern. We agreed on a concrete signal: they would pause after each point in meetings to invite others to respond.

*Result:* Over the following weeks the team dynamic shifted noticeably. Junior engineers told me they felt more comfortable speaking up. The senior engineer later said the conversation helped them become a better tech lead.

---

Q: Describe a situation where your team missed a deadline. What happened and what did you learn?

*Situation:* My team was building a new payments integration. A few weeks before launch we discovered a third-party API had undocumented rate limits that broke our architecture.

*Task:* I had to manage the slip transparently while keeping stakeholders confident and the team focused.

*Action:* I called an immediate sync with product and business stakeholders to explain the root cause clearly, without assigning blame. I gave a revised estimate with clear milestones and proposed a phased rollout to deliver partial value sooner. Inside the team I restructured priorities so part of the group focused on the rate-limit workaround while the rest continued on unblocked work.

*Result:* We shipped a limited version on the original date and the full integration followed shortly after. Stakeholders cited the transparency as a highlight, and we added a third-party API audit step to our project checklist going forward.

---

Q: How do you build trust with a new team when you join as their manager?

*Situation:* I joined a group of engineers mid-year after their previous manager had left abruptly, leaving uncertainty about direction and priorities.

*Task:* I needed to stabilise the team quickly and earn their trust without pretending I had all the answers.

*Action:* In the first few weeks I ran one-on-ones with every team member, asking what was working, what was not, and what each person wanted from their manager. I listened far more than I spoke. I then shared back a summary of what I heard (anonymised), showing the team I had actually absorbed their input. I avoided making big structural changes early, focusing instead on removing blockers they had flagged for months. Once I had enough context, I worked with the team to agree on a single clear quarterly goal.

*Result:* Team engagement improved visibly. In a follow-up pulse check run by HR, scores for manager effectiveness were meaningfully higher than the previous cycle. Engineers who had been considering leaving told me they had decided to stay.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

The STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is the baseline, but Airbnb interviewers typically probe beyond the surface story. Expect follow-up questions like 'what would you do differently?' or 'how did the other person feel?' Prepare a second layer for each story.

For leadership questions, structure your answer around: the context you inherited or created, the specific decision you made and why, how you brought others along, and the measurable or observable outcome.

For conflict and stakeholder questions, use a three-part frame: acknowledge the tension honestly, describe the actions you took to understand all sides, and explain how you moved toward resolution without avoiding the hard part.

For 'how do you approach X?' questions (roadmap planning, hiring, performance management), give a principle first, then a concrete example. Airbnb interviewers tend to find abstract frameworks hollow without a real story to anchor them.

On length: candidates report that Airbnb interviewers prefer focused, concise answers. If you are talking for a long stretch, you are likely over-explaining the situation and under-explaining your actions and results. Aim to keep each answer tight enough that the interviewer can ask meaningful follow-ups.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Airbnb EM interviewers are looking for a few things that show up consistently across candidate reports.

Empathy as a leadership tool, not a buzzword. Interviewers probe whether you genuinely understand the people you manage. Vague statements about 'caring for the team' do not land. Specific examples of adjusting your style for an individual engineer, or of catching a problem early because you were paying attention, resonate strongly.

Comfort with ambiguity. Airbnb operates in a fast-changing market. They want evidence you can set direction without perfect information and keep a team moving when priorities shift.

Cross-functional credibility. Engineering Managers here work closely with product, design, and data teams. Interviewers look for candidates who can tell a compelling story from a product perspective, not just an engineering one.

Ownership over process. They are not interested in managers who hide behind process. They want to hear that you took personal responsibility for outcomes, including the ones that did not go as planned.

Depth over breadth. Airbnb interviewers typically prefer a single well-developed story over several shallow ones. Pick your best examples and know them in detail.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Week 1: Research and story bank
Read Airbnb's recent engineering blog posts and any publicly available talks from their engineering leaders. Note recurring themes around reliability, scale, and culture. Write down several career stories covering leadership moments, failures, conflicts, and technical decisions. Tag each story so you know which question types it can answer.

Week 2: Practice with structure
Run through each of the questions listed above using the STAR method. Record yourself or practice with a peer. Check that your answers do not contain vague phrases like 'we worked together' or 'things improved.' Every answer needs a concrete action and a specific result.

Week 3: Cross-functional and product depth
Practice explaining engineering trade-offs in product terms. Pick a few of Airbnb's known product areas (search ranking, trust and safety, payments) and think about how your past work connects to similar problems.

Before each round: Candidates typically report having some time between rounds to collect their thoughts. Keep a brief running log of stories you have already used so you do not repeat the same example in back-to-back interviews.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Answering 'we' when they asked about 'you'. Interviewers at Airbnb specifically want to know your contribution. If every answer starts with 'the team did...', they cannot assess you. Be specific about what you decided and what you personally did.

Leading with technical detail instead of leadership. You are interviewing for a people manager role. Spending most of your answer explaining the architecture instead of how you led the team through a challenge misses the point.

Generic culture statements. Saying 'I believe in psychological safety' without a supporting story reads as preparation without substance. Every principle you state needs a real example behind it.

Not preparing questions for the interviewer. Candidates report that Airbnb interviewers pay attention to the quality of your questions. Questions that show you have done your research (about team structure, current engineering challenges, or how success is measured) land better than generic ones.

Underselling failures. Airbnb's culture values honesty and learning. A candidate who presents every story as a smooth success comes across as either lucky or not self-aware. Show that you can analyse what went wrong and what you changed.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

How many rounds does the Airbnb Engineering Manager interview typically have?

Candidates report that the process typically includes a recruiter screen, a hiring manager conversation, and a panel of interviews covering leadership, cross-functional collaboration, and sometimes a technical or system design discussion. The exact number of rounds can vary by team and seniority level. Budget for several conversations in total as a practical planning guide.

Does Airbnb ask technical questions in the EM interview?

At the Engineering Manager level, candidates report that technical discussions focus more on system design, architectural trade-offs, and how you make technical decisions as a leader rather than on coding. You may be asked to walk through a past technical decision your team made and explain the reasoning behind it. Pure coding screens are uncommon for EM roles but are occasionally reported at earlier stages in the process.

What salary can I expect for an Engineering Manager role at Airbnb in India?

Knok jobradar data shows Engineering Manager roles in India ranging from 35-60 LPA at the Manager level, 55-90 LPA at Senior Manager, and 90-150+ LPA at Director level. Total compensation including equity and bonuses is publicly reported to be meaningfully above base; check levels.fyi for current data points, noting the sample sizes listed there.

How should I prepare for Airbnb's culture-fit questions?

Airbnb publicly describes its culture around belonging, connection, and empathy. Interviewers probe whether you actually live these values rather than just mention them. Prepare a few specific stories where you demonstrated inclusive leadership, navigated a difficult interpersonal situation, or made a decision that prioritised team health alongside business outcomes. Generic statements without real examples rarely satisfy Airbnb interviewers.

How long does the Airbnb EM hiring process take from first contact to offer?

Candidates report timelines that typically range from a few weeks to a couple of months, depending on team urgency and scheduling availability. The process can move faster when there is active headcount pressure. It is reasonable to ask your recruiter for a rough timeline after the hiring manager call so you can plan around other offers you may have in play.

Is there a case study or take-home assignment as part of the process?

Some candidates report receiving a written exercise related to team planning, roadmap prioritisation, or an engineering leadership scenario. This is not universal across all EM roles at Airbnb and varies by team. Confirm with your recruiter early on whether a written component is included for your specific role so you are not caught off guard.
