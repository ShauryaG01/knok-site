01 Overview

Overview

cursor is the AI-first code editor that professional developers worldwide use to write, edit, and debug code with large language model assistance. The company moves fast, ships often, and expects Engineering Managers to be genuine technical partners to their teams, not just people coordinators. As of July 2026, cursor had 119 open roles across the organisation, with Engineering Manager positions among the most competitive.

Candidates report a process that typically spans a recruiter screen, a technical leadership discussion, a behavioural depth interview, and a final panel with cross-functional stakeholders. Rounds are not always named formally, so expect one conversation to shift between people management, technical judgment, and product thinking. The process is generally faster-moving than at large enterprise companies, and directness is valued throughout.

If you are applying from India, Bangalore leads with 182 open Engineering Manager positions across companies tracked on knok jobradar as of July 2026, making it the strongest hiring city for this role in the country.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

cursor interviews for Engineering Managers probe three areas: technical leadership, people management, and product sense in the context of AI developer tooling. These are the questions candidates most commonly report encountering.

  1. How do you balance shipping velocity with code quality on a team building AI-powered developer tools?
  2. Tell me about a time your team disagreed on a technical direction and how you resolved it.
  3. How do you hire and develop senior engineers who have deeper domain expertise than you in their area?
  4. cursor's product changes rapidly as the underlying models improve. How do you keep a team aligned and motivated through constant change?
  5. Describe a time you managed underperformance on a team without hurting morale.
  6. How do you think about technical debt in a company that ships at high speed?
  7. Give an example of how you improved cross-team collaboration on a shared platform or infrastructure problem.
  8. How do you decide when to build something in-house versus using a third-party service or model?
  9. Walk me through how you set goals for your team and track progress over a quarter.
  10. How have you evolved as a manager since your first people management role?
  11. cursor cares deeply about developer experience. How do you measure and improve your own team's productivity?
  12. Tell me about a time you pushed back on a product or business decision and how you handled the conversation.

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Use the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result) for every behavioural question. Here are three worked examples tailored to cursor's context.

---

Q: Tell me about a time your team disagreed on a technical direction and how you resolved it.

*Situation:* My team was split on whether to use a fully managed infrastructure service or build a custom in-house solution for our ML inference pipeline. Both sides had strong technical opinions and the debate was going in circles.

*Task:* I needed to make a call quickly because the delay was blocking several downstream teams and we were losing credibility with partner teams.

*Action:* I ran a focused decision session where each engineer had a short slot to present the trade-offs they cared about most, mapped against our near-term roadmap. Rather than picking a side outright, I proposed a middle path: adopt the managed option to unblock everyone immediately, with an explicit re-evaluation checkpoint after the first production release when we would have real usage data.

*Result:* The team aligned and we shipped on schedule. The re-evaluation gave us real data to make a better long-term call together. The engineers who had favoured the custom build said they felt heard, and one of them led the re-evaluation themselves.

---

Q: Describe a time you managed underperformance without hurting team morale.

*Situation:* A senior engineer on my team was consistently missing commitments. The rest of the team had quietly started working around them, which was creating uneven load and low-level resentment.

*Task:* I needed to address this directly without creating fear across the team or letting the situation grow into a culture problem.

*Action:* I had a candid one-on-one, shared specific examples tied to sprint outcomes, and worked with the engineer to identify root causes together. We built a concrete improvement plan with weekly check-ins. I told the broader team I was handling a performance situation without sharing private details, so they knew it was not being ignored.

*Result:* The engineer's output and reliability improved clearly over the following quarter. The team's trust in my willingness to handle hard conversations grew, and the informal workarounds disappeared.

---

Q: How do you balance shipping velocity with code quality?

*Situation:* At a previous company, we were under significant pressure to ship a major product feature ahead of a competitor launch.

*Task:* I needed to maintain pace without letting the codebase degrade to a point where future work would slow down significantly.

*Action:* I introduced a 'tech debt budget' practice where each sprint explicitly reserved capacity for quality work and cleanup. I worked with the team to agree on which shortcuts were acceptable short-term and which were not. Every fast-follow cleanup item was logged and scheduled, not just verbally promised.

*Result:* We shipped the feature on time and the codebase stayed in good shape. The practice spread to other teams in the org after they saw how it worked.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

The STAR method is the baseline for all behavioural questions at cursor. Every answer needs a concrete Situation, a clear Task you personally owned, specific Actions you took (say 'I', not 'we'), and a measurable or observable Result.

For technical direction questions, use a simple 'options, trade-offs, decision' structure. Name two or three options you considered, explain the key trade-offs in plain terms, and explain your reasoning for the choice you made. cursor interviewers are engineers themselves and will press follow-up questions if your answers stay vague.

For people questions, lead with empathy and then move to structure. Show that you understood the human situation first, then describe the process you used. Avoid sounding like you applied a generic HR process without actually thinking.

For product sense questions, show that you use or have studied cursor closely. Talk about the developer loop: edit, build, test, debug. Frame your thinking around reducing friction in that loop, because that is cursor's core value proposition.

On specifics: interviewers at AI product companies appreciate concrete detail. Where you have real outcomes from your own experience, use them. Where you are estimating or hedging, say so directly.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

cursor is building a product used by senior engineers every day, so the bar for technical credibility in an EM is high. Interviewers are typically looking for four things.

Technical depth without micromanagement. They want to see that you can engage meaningfully in architecture and design discussions, set the right technical direction, and then trust your team to execute. They are not looking for someone who needs to approve every code review.

Speed and pragmatism. cursor ships fast. Interviewers want evidence that you have made hard trade-offs under time pressure and owned the outcome rather than deferring it upward or sideways.

People clarity. They want managers who are honest about performance, direct in feedback, and proactive about developing their team. Vague or overly diplomatic answers to people questions are a red flag.

Product empathy for developers. Because the product is a code editor, you should understand the developer experience deeply. Candidates who have actually used cursor and can speak to what they noticed, what they liked, and what they would change tend to stand out.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

In your first week: know the product and the company. Use cursor daily for at least a week before your interview. Read any public engineering blog posts or talks from the team. Understand at a conceptual level how the product uses LLMs. Note two or three things you would change or improve and be ready to discuss them.

In your second week: build your story bank. Write out several STAR stories from your career that cover: a technical decision under pressure, an underperformance situation, a cross-team collaboration challenge, a hiring decision, and a product trade-off you owned. For each story, identify the specific result and what you personally learned.

In your third week: practise delivery. Record yourself answering questions out loud. EM interviews at fast-moving AI companies often move quickly, so practise being concise. Aim to cover the key points and then invite follow-up rather than over-explaining.

Before each round: review the job description, map your stories to the listed responsibilities, and prepare two or three thoughtful questions for the interviewer. Questions about how technical decisions get made, how EMs are measured, and what the biggest current challenges are for the team tend to land well.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Talking about 'we' instead of 'I'. Interviewers are assessing your personal judgment and actions. Every answer should make clear what you specifically decided, said, or did.

Being vague about results. Saying a project 'went well' or 'the team was happy' is weak. Name the actual outcome: shipped on schedule, a specific bottleneck was removed, team retention improved.

Over-indexing on process. Describing a perfect agile or OKR process in detail is not what cursor interviewers want. They want to see your thinking and judgment, not your adherence to a methodology.

Not knowing the product. Candidates who have not used cursor before the interview are noticeable immediately. This is one of the easiest preparation steps and one of the most commonly skipped.

Giving generic answers to company-specific questions. When asked about managing a team through rapid change (a cursor reality as models evolve quickly), give a specific example from your own career. A theoretical answer about change management will not land.

Softening failures too much. If asked about a mistake or a conflict, do not smooth it over until there is no real lesson left. Interviewers want evidence that you can face hard situations clearly and learn from them.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

How many rounds does a cursor Engineering Manager interview typically have?

Candidates report that the process typically includes a recruiter screen, one or two technical leadership discussions, a behavioural depth round, and a final panel. The exact structure can vary by team and seniority level, so it is worth asking your recruiter once you are in the process. The overall timeline is generally faster than at large enterprise companies.

Does cursor expect Engineering Managers to write code?

cursor does not typically require EMs to write production code, but strong technical credibility is expected. You should be able to engage in architecture and design discussions, evaluate trade-offs clearly, and earn the trust of senior engineers on your team. Candidates who can speak fluently about how AI code tools work at a conceptual level tend to perform better in the technical leadership rounds.

What salary can an Engineering Manager expect at cursor?

cursor does not publish salary bands publicly, and India-specific compensation data is limited. Based on industry surveys and publicly reported ranges, Engineering Manager roles at AI product companies in India typically fall in the 35-60 LPA band, Senior Manager roles in the 55-90 LPA band, and Director-level roles at 90 LPA and above. Actual offers depend on level, location, equity component, and negotiation, so treat these as directional figures.

How important is an AI or ML background for this role?

You do not need to be an ML researcher to be an Engineering Manager at cursor, but you should understand how large language models are used in developer tools at a product level. Being able to discuss context windows, latency trade-offs, and how model updates affect product behaviour will help in interviews. cursor's product is built on AI, so EMs are expected to think about how model improvements change engineering priorities over time.

Are there cursor Engineering Manager roles open for candidates based in India?

cursor's hiring is primarily US-based, though the company does list remote-eligible roles. As of July 2026, cursor had 119 open roles tracked across levels. If you are India-based, confirm remote eligibility with the recruiter at your first screen. For the broader EM market in India, Bangalore leads with 182 open positions across companies in the sector.

What is the best way to prepare if I have never managed an AI product team before?

Focus on transferable leadership skills and genuine curiosity about AI tooling. Use cursor extensively before your interview and form your own views on what works and what could be better. In your STAR stories, look for examples where you led a team through rapid technical change or ambiguous product direction. Being honest about what you have not done, combined with clear thinking about how you would approach it, is valued more than overstating your experience.
