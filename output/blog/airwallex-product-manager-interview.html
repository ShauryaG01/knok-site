01 Overview

Overview

Airwallex is a global fintech company building payment infrastructure, multi-currency business accounts, and embedded finance tools for companies moving money across borders. Their product teams work on hard B2B problems, payment rails, FX, compliance workflows, and developer APIs used by businesses of every size. As of July 2026, Airwallex has 605 open roles globally. PM interviews here are typically more technical and domain-heavy than at consumer-facing companies. Candidates typically report a process that includes a recruiter screen, a product case study or take-home, product thinking and behavioural rounds, and a cross-functional panel, though the exact structure varies by team and seniority. 02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

Airwallex interviewers typically probe for payments domain knowledge, B2B product thinking, and the ability to navigate compliance constraints. Based on what candidates report, these questions come up often: 1. Walk us through a payments or fintech product you shipped, what was the problem, your role, and the outcome.
2. How would you improve Airwallex's multi-currency business account for Indian exporters or SMBs?
3. A merchant reports that international payouts are failing at a high rate. How do you diagnose and fix it?
4. How do you balance shipping fast with compliance and regulatory requirements in a financial product?
5. How would you design a north-star metric for Airwallex's FX conversion product?
6. Describe a time you had to push back on a large enterprise client's feature request to protect the broader roadmap.
7. How would you prioritise features for Airwallex's embedded finance API vs. its business-facing dashboard?
8. Tell me about a time you worked with engineering to reduce payment failure rates or settlement delays.
9. A well-funded competitor announces zero-fee international transfers. How do you respond as a PM?
10. How do you think about trust and fraud in a cross-border payments context, and how does that shape product design?
11. Describe how you would approach launching a financial product in a new market where Airwallex has limited regulatory presence.
12. How do you gather and prioritise insights from business customers who have very different needs, solopreneurs vs. large enterprises?

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Use the STAR format for all behavioural questions. Here are three worked examples tailored to Airwallex's context. --- Q: Describe a time you built or improved a product feature under compliance constraints. *Situation:* At my previous company, we were building a cross-border payout feature for freelancers receiving overseas payments. Midway through development, our compliance team flagged that additional KYC steps were required by regulation for transactions above a certain threshold. *Task:* I needed to redesign the payment flow to include these checks without killing conversion for legitimate users who were already frustrated by verification friction. *Action:* I ran a discovery sprint, interviewed a group of affected users, mapped the compliance requirements with our legal team, and worked with design on a tiered verification flow: light-touch for lower-value transactions, and a guided document-upload step for higher-value ones. I added clear in-app messaging framing the extra step as user protection rather than a hurdle. *Result:* We shipped on the regulatory deadline. Drop-off on the payout flow stayed flat compared to our pre-compliance baseline, and the compliance team flagged our messaging approach as a template for future features. --- Q: Tell me about a time you used data to make a product decision that turned out to need revisiting. *Situation:* I was PM for a business account onboarding flow. Funnel data showed a steep drop-off at the 'add your first team member' step, so I pushed to make that step optional and shift it post-activation. *Task:* I owned the A/B test design and rollout, and needed to align engineering and growth on the hypothesis. *Action:* We ran a four-week test and saw a short-term lift in activation rates. I called the test a win and we shipped. Three months later, retention data showed that accounts who skipped the team-member step had significantly lower 90-day retention, they were not using the collaborative features that drove long-term stickiness. *Result:* I re-examined the data, acknowledged the mistake in a retro, and we redesigned the step as a 'soft nudge with skip' option. The following cohort recovered the retention gap. The learning: optimise for the right downstream metric, not the first funnel metric that moves. --- Q: How did you handle a conflict between a key enterprise client's request and your product roadmap? *Situation:* A top enterprise account asked us to build a custom reconciliation report format, not on the roadmap and estimated at two weeks of engineering time. *Task:* I had to balance the commercial relationship against the risk of one-off builds that would not scale. *Action:* I met the client's finance team to understand the underlying need. They needed a specific column order to match their internal ERP system. I worked with engineering to build a configurable export-settings feature, a two-day effort, that let any client reorder columns without custom code. I framed this to the client as a platform investment, not a bespoke fix. *Result:* The client renewed their contract satisfied with the solution. Within two quarters, three other enterprise accounts used the same settings feature, confirming it was a real product gap and not a one-off ask.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

For behavioural questions, STAR
Always close the Result with a measurable outcome. If you cannot share exact figures, describe the direction of change and why it mattered to the business. For product design / improvement questions, Jobs-to-be-Done + North Star
1. Clarify: who is the business customer, and what job are they trying to do?
2. Map the pain: where does the current experience break down for them?
3. Define success: what is the one metric that proves this is working?
4. Prioritise solutions: impact vs. effort, with explicit attention to regulatory feasibility. For metric or diagnostic questions, Data → Insight → Decision
1. Break the metric into components (e.g. payment success rate = total attempts minus failures, segmented by error type and corridor).
2. State your hypothesis for the root cause.
3. Describe what data you would pull and who you would speak to.
4. Explain the decision you would make and how you would validate it. For 'competitor does X' questions, Strategy Anchor
Start by restating Airwallex's positioning and who their core customer is. Then evaluate whether the competitor move threatens that core. Avoid knee-jerk feature matching, interviewers want to see you anchor on strategy, not react to tactics.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Airwallex PMs work at the intersection of finance, engineering, compliance, and global business operations. Interviewers are typically looking for: Payments and fintech fluency, You do not need to have worked at a payments company, but you should be able to speak to concepts like payment rails, settlement, FX spreads, and the role of a PSP at a basic level. Candidates who have only worked on consumer apps often struggle here. B2B empathy, Airwallex's customers are businesses, not individuals. Interviewers want to see that you understand multi-stakeholder buying, and that a reliable payout matters more to a business customer than a polished animation. Comfort with compliance as a constraint, Financial products are shaped by regulation. Interviewers want PMs who can hold both 'what does the user want' and 'what can we legally build' in mind at the same time, not treat compliance as a footnote. Data discipline, Expect to walk through how you define metrics, run experiments, and interpret results. Vague answers about 'I look at the data' will not land well. Cross-functional credibility, Airwallex teams are global and cross-functional. Show that you can drive alignment across engineering, legal, design, and sales without relying on authority to do it.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Two to three weeks out
- Use Airwallex's product yourself or watch demo walkthroughs to understand the business account, card, and payout products first-hand.
- Build your payments vocabulary: how correspondent banking works, what settlement lag means, why FX rates vary by corridor, and what PCI-DSS means for product decisions.
- Check publicly reported interview experiences on Glassdoor to get a sense of question types and difficulty levels candidates describe. One to two weeks out
- Practise two to three product improvement answers using the Jobs-to-be-Done framework, specifically for B2B fintech scenarios.
- Prepare five to six STAR stories covering: shipping under constraints, data-driven decisions, stakeholder management, cross-functional alignment, and a product failure or learning.
- Study Airwallex's recent product announcements and blog posts, interviewers often ask 'what would you improve about our product?' and notice when candidates have done real homework. Final week
- Do mock interviews with someone who can give honest pushback on your metric and case answers.
- Prepare two to three sharp questions for your interviewers about team structure, how roadmap decisions interact with compliance, or how success is measured in the role.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Treating it like a consumer app interview, Airwallex's customers are businesses. Answers focused purely on delightful consumer UX without addressing reliability, reconciliation, or trust will not resonate with an Airwallex interviewer. Skipping the 'why this matters for Airwallex' step, When answering a product design question, always anchor on who Airwallex serves and what their business model is. Generic PM frameworks without that context come across as copy-paste prep. Treating compliance as a blocker to mention and move past, Airwallex interviewers want to see you treat regulation as a product variable you actively design around, not a constraint you acknowledge once and ignore. Vague STAR answers, Saying 'I worked closely with the team and we improved the metric' is not a STAR answer. Name the specific action you personally took, the constraint you faced, and the measurable change that resulted. Not knowing the company's product surface, Airwallex has distinct product lines: multi-currency wallets, global cards, payout APIs, embedded finance. Candidates who show up without knowing the difference between these tend to struggle in 'improve our product' questions. Over-indexing on speed without acknowledging trust, In fintech PM interviews, saying 'I would ship fast and iterate' without acknowledging that financial errors erode customer trust is a red flag. Show that you understand the trade-off explicitly.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

Does Airwallex prefer candidates with prior fintech experience?

Candidates report that fintech or payments experience is a strong plus but not always a strict requirement, particularly at mid-level PM roles. What matters more is demonstrating that you can learn the domain quickly and apply product thinking to financial constraints. Coming in with a self-taught understanding of cross-border payments will go a long way toward bridging the gap.

What salary can I expect as a PM at Airwallex in India?

Based on knok jobradar data, PM salaries in India broadly range from 12-20 LPA at Associate PM level, 24-40 LPA for PM roles with three to six years of experience, 40-60 LPA for Senior PMs, and 55-90+ LPA for Group or Principal PMs. Airwallex-specific figures are not independently verified at scale, so check Glassdoor and levels.fyi for role-specific data points and note the sample sizes when comparing.

How long does the Airwallex PM interview process typically take?

Candidates typically report a process spanning two to four weeks from initial screen to offer, though this varies by team, level, and hiring urgency. The process commonly includes a recruiter screen, a product case or take-home, one or two product and behavioural interviews, and a final panel. Do not assume rounds are standardised across teams, ask your recruiter for the specific structure for the role you applied to.

What kind of product case study does Airwallex typically give?

Candidates report case studies that are usually B2B and fintech-adjacent, for example, improving a payout product, designing a merchant dashboard feature, or diagnosing a drop in payment success rates. Publicly reported cases tend to reward structured thinking and domain awareness over generic frameworks. Practise cases that involve regulatory trade-offs, not just user delight.

How important are SQL or data skills for a PM role at Airwallex?

Airwallex is a data-driven company and interviewers commonly assess whether PMs can work independently with data. You are unlikely to be asked to write complex queries live, but candidates report being expected to explain how they would pull and interpret data to drive decisions. Comfort with metrics, funnel analysis, and A/B test design matters more than raw SQL fluency.

Should I apply to Airwallex if I have only worked at B2C companies?

Yes, but come prepared to bridge the gap. The most common feedback from candidates who did not advance is that their examples skewed too consumer-facing. Prepare at least two to three stories where your decisions considered multi-stakeholder buying, reliability over visual polish, or enterprise-specific constraints. Demonstrating that you understand the B2B vs. B2C product difference matters more than the company name on your resume.
