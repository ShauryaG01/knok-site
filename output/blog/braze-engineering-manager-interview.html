01 Overview

Overview

Braze is a customer engagement platform used by global brands to deliver personalised push notifications, email, SMS, and in-app messages at scale. Their engineering teams build the real-time data pipelines and APIs that power billions of customer interactions, so technical credibility matters even for managers. An Engineering Manager at Braze is expected to lead a team of software engineers, own delivery of complex features, partner closely with product managers and designers, and grow individual contributors toward senior levels.

Braze currently has 252 open roles tracked on knok jobradar (as of July 2026), reflecting strong hiring momentum across the organisation. EM roles in India are most concentrated in Bangalore, based on market data for the Engineering Manager role category.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

Here are 12 questions candidates commonly report facing in Braze Engineering Manager interviews.

People Leadership

  1. Tell me about a time you delivered difficult feedback to a senior engineer. How did you approach it and what changed after?
  2. How do you identify and develop high-potential engineers on your team? Walk me through a specific example.
  3. Describe a situation where two engineers on your team had a conflict that was affecting delivery. What did you do?
  4. How do you handle an engineer who is consistently missing expectations despite coaching?

Delivery and Execution

  1. Tell me about a project that slipped its timeline. How did you catch it, what did you do, and what did you learn?
  2. How do you balance technical debt reduction against shipping new features when your product manager is pushing hard on roadmap?
  3. Describe how you handled a high-severity production incident as the engineering leader on call.

Technical and Architecture

  1. Braze processes messages at very high throughput in near real time. What architectural trade-offs would you consider when designing a system that needs to handle message delivery spikes?
  2. How do you stay technically sharp as a manager without becoming a bottleneck in design decisions?

Cross-Functional and Strategy

  1. Tell me about a time you pushed back on a product requirement. How did you make your case and what was the outcome?
  2. How do you align your team's quarterly goals with broader company objectives when the company strategy shifts mid-cycle?
  3. How do you think about hiring? Walk me through how you built or scaled an engineering team in the past.

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Below are three STAR-format answers to questions commonly asked in Braze EM interviews. Adapt the specifics to your own experience.

Q: Tell me about a time you delivered difficult feedback to a senior engineer.

*Situation:* I managed a senior engineer who consistently produced high-quality individual work but dismissed junior engineers' suggestions in team meetings, which was visibly affecting morale.

*Task:* I needed to address the behaviour without damaging our relationship or losing a technically strong contributor.

*Action:* I set up a one-on-one and opened with specific observations: 'In the last two sprint reviews, I noticed you interrupted Riya twice before she could finish her point, and the team looked hesitant to speak after that.' I avoided labels and focused on impact. I then asked for his perspective, which revealed he felt pressure to move fast and did not realise how he was coming across. We agreed on a concrete change: he would ask one clarifying question before responding in design discussions.

*Result:* Over the following month, two junior engineers independently mentioned that meetings felt more open. The senior engineer later said it was the most useful feedback he had received in years. I used this to build a 'listen first' norm into our team working agreements.

---

Q: Tell me about a project that slipped its timeline.

*Situation:* We were building an in-app messaging feature and three sprints in, I noticed velocity had dropped but no one had flagged a risk.

*Task:* I needed to understand the root cause, communicate honestly with stakeholders, and recover what we could of the original timeline.

*Action:* I ran a quick no-blame diagnosis with the tech lead and found two causes: an undiscovered dependency on a third-party SDK and two engineers blocked on unclear acceptance criteria. I moved those engineers to unblocked tasks, escalated the SDK dependency to our platform team, and sent a transparent update to stakeholders with a revised scope and a date I was confident we could hit.

*Result:* We shipped a reduced-scope version on the revised date and the remaining features in the following sprint. The PM appreciated the early transparency. We added a dependency-mapping step to our project kickoff checklist to prevent recurrence.

---

Q: How do you stay technically sharp as a manager without becoming a bottleneck?

*Situation:* When I moved from senior engineer to manager, I worried about losing technical credibility while also not wanting every design decision to wait for my approval.

*Task:* I needed to stay informed enough to guide and challenge the team while keeping decision-making at the right level.

*Action:* I introduced a lightweight design review practice where engineers share a one-page design doc before coding, and I joined reviews as a participant rather than an approver. I also blocked time each week to read engineering case studies from companies with similar infrastructure challenges, and I paired occasionally with engineers on complex debugging sessions.

*Result:* Engineers felt trusted to own decisions. When I did raise a concern in a design review, it landed because it was specific. My skip-level manager noted that my team shipped with fewer late-stage architecture surprises than peer teams.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

Three frameworks that work well in Braze EM interviews:

Impact-then-process for leadership questions. Start with what changed (the outcome or impact) and then explain how you got there. Interviewers at product companies like Braze care about results, so leading with impact signals that you connect your actions to business and people outcomes rather than treating process as an end in itself.

Three-level framing for technical questions. When asked about system design or architecture trade-offs: first describe the constraint (scale, latency, cost), then describe two or three options with their trade-offs, then explain which you would choose and why for that specific context. This shows structured thinking rather than a single 'right answer' mindset, which matters at a company handling real-time message delivery at high throughput.

Listen-align-decide for cross-functional conflict questions. When describing a disagreement with a PM or stakeholder: show that you listened to understand their constraint, found where your goals aligned, and then explained your reasoning before deciding or escalating. Braze is a collaborative culture and interviewers look for managers who influence without authority.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Braze interviewers for EM roles typically look for five qualities, based on what candidates report after going through the process.

Customer obsession rooted in engineering. Braze's product directly affects whether a brand's message reaches a customer at the right moment. Interviewers want to see that you understand the downstream impact of engineering decisions on end users, not just internal metrics.

Candour and directness. Braze values honest communication in both directions. Expect questions that probe whether you give timely, specific feedback and whether you surface bad news to stakeholders early rather than late.

Comfort with ambiguity at scale. Many interview questions involve under-defined problems. Interviewers want to see you structure the problem, identify what you know and do not know, and make a reasonable call without waiting for perfect information.

A multiplier mindset. Strong answers show how you made the people around you more effective, not just how you personally solved something.

Technical credibility without micromanagement. You do not need to write production code as an EM, but you need enough depth to challenge designs, spot risks, and earn the respect of senior engineers.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

A focused plan for the two to three weeks before your Braze EM interview:

Week 1: Know Braze deeply. Read Braze's engineering blog and product documentation to understand how their platform works. Study their core concepts (Canvases, Currents, Connected Content). Be ready to speak fluently about customer engagement infrastructure trade-offs, particularly around real-time message delivery and data pipelines at scale.

Week 2: Build your story bank. List eight to ten leadership situations from your career covering: difficult feedback, project recovery, hiring decisions, cross-functional conflicts, and technical trade-offs. Write each one in STAR format and trim it to under two minutes when spoken aloud.

Week 3: Practice out loud. Preparation that stays in your head does not transfer to a real conversation. Do mock interviews with a peer or record yourself answering questions. Focus on leading with impact and cutting filler words.

Throughout: align on scope. Understand the team size and product area for the specific role you are applying to. Prepare questions for interviewers that show you are thinking about how to be effective from day one, not just whether you will receive an offer.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Staying too abstract. The most common mistake in EM interviews is giving high-level answers without specific situations. 'I always give regular feedback to my team' tells an interviewer nothing. A real situation with names, context, and outcome is what lands.

Taking all the credit. Strong EM candidates describe what the team achieved, not what they personally built. Interviewers notice when 'I' dominates an answer about team delivery.

Ignoring the customer angle. Braze is a B2B product company. Candidates who frame engineering decisions purely in terms of technical elegance, without connecting them to customer outcomes, miss what Braze values most.

Skipping the result. Many candidates describe the situation and the action in detail and then trail off before stating the result. Always close the loop: what happened, what changed, and what you would do differently.

Not asking about the team. Candidates who ask no questions, or who only ask about compensation, signal low curiosity. Ask about the team's current challenges, how success is measured in the first six months, and what the biggest technical investment is for the coming year.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

How many rounds does the Braze EM interview typically have?

Candidates typically report four to six rounds, covering an initial recruiter screen, a hiring manager conversation, a leadership and behavioural loop, a technical or system design round, and a final executive conversation. Some candidates report a take-home or case study component as well. The exact structure varies by team and level, so confirm the format with your recruiter early in the process.

Do I need to write code in the Braze EM interview?

Candidates typically report that live coding is not required for EM roles at Braze, but system design and technical discussion rounds are common. You should be comfortable discussing architecture trade-offs, scalability decisions, and engineering quality practices at a detailed level. Being able to engage credibly with senior engineers on technical topics is expected, even if you do not write production code day to day.

What salary can I expect for an EM role at Braze in India?

Based on knok jobradar market data, Engineering Manager roles in India range from 35-60 LPA at the Manager level and 55-90 LPA at the Senior Manager level. Braze-specific compensation for India is not publicly reported at scale, so Glassdoor and levels.fyi are the best sources for community-reported figures. Total compensation at product companies typically includes stock and performance bonus on top of base, so factor those components in when evaluating any offer.

How should I prepare for Braze's values and culture fit?

Braze does not publish a formal list of leadership principles the way some other companies do, but candidates report that themes of customer obsession, candour, and high engineering standards come up consistently across rounds. Build a story bank of eight to ten career situations that reflect these qualities and be ready to adapt them to specific questions. Reading Braze's engineering blog before interviews helps you speak their language and reference real product context.

Based on knok jobradar data as of July 2026, Braze has 252 open roles tracked across the company, reflecting active hiring momentum. In the broader market, Bangalore accounts for the largest share of Engineering Manager openings in India, with 182 EM roles tracked across all companies. How long does the Braze hiring process take from first screen to offer?

Candidates report that the full process typically takes three to six weeks from the initial recruiter call to a final decision. Timelines can vary depending on interviewer availability and how quickly feedback is collected across rounds. Following up politely with your recruiter after each stage is a reasonable way to stay informed and signal continued interest without being intrusive.
