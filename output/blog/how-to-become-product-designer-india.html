01 Role Overview

Role Overview

A product designer shapes how digital products look, feel, and work. You research what users need, sketch ideas, build prototypes in Figma, and hand off polished specs to engineers. In India, product designers work across funded startups, large tech firms, and agencies building apps and SaaS tools. The role sits at the crossroads of user empathy, visual craft, and business thinking. Companies expect you to run usability tests, collaborate with product managers, and keep designs consistent across a product. If you enjoy solving real problems through visual thinking, this career rewards both creativity and logic.

02 Skills You Need

Skills You Need

Core design tools
- Figma: the industry standard for wireframes, prototypes, and design systems. Learn it first.
- Adobe XD or Sketch: less common now but still used in some agencies.

Design fundamentals
- Typography, colour theory, spacing, and grid layouts.
- Component-based thinking for building reusable UI elements.

User research
- User interviews, usability testing, affinity mapping.
- Writing a clear research brief and summarising findings for PMs.

Prototyping
- Clickable prototypes in Figma.
- Basic micro-interaction logic (flows, transitions).

Collaboration skills
- Presenting designs to stakeholders and explaining your decisions.
- Working with engineers, understanding what is easy or hard to build.
- Handing off specs via Figma's developer mode.

Nice to have
- Basic HTML/CSS so you can speak the engineer's language.
- Familiarity with analytics tools like Mixpanel or Hotjar to back design decisions with data.

03 Step By Step Path

Step By Step Path

Step 1: Understand what product designers actually do
Spend a couple of weeks reading case studies on Behance, Dribbble, and Medium. Follow designers at Indian companies on LinkedIn. Notice that the job is less about 'making things pretty' and more about solving user problems within real constraints.

Step 2: Learn the fundamentals
Pick up a structured course covering UX process and visual design basics. Google's UX Design Certificate on Coursera is a widely used starting point. Pair it with a free Figma tutorial series on YouTube. Focus on the design process: research, define, ideate, prototype, test.

Step 3: Master Figma
Figma is non-negotiable. Build small practice projects: redesign an app you use daily, design a simple sign-up flow, create a basic component library. Do this alongside your course, not after.

Step 4: Do your first real project
Pick a real problem (a local shop's ordering flow, a college portal that frustrates students) and solve it end to end. Run a quick user interview with three to five people. Build a wireframe, then a prototype. Document your process and your decisions. This becomes your first portfolio case study.

Step 5: Build a portfolio with two to three case studies
Quality beats quantity. Each case study should show the problem, your research, your process, your solution, and what you would do differently. Write in plain English. Recruiters scan portfolios quickly, so make the story easy to follow.

Step 6: Apply early and learn from rejections
Start applying to internships or junior roles once you have two solid case studies. Many candidates apply only when they feel 'ready.' Apply early instead. Rejection emails usually tell you what to fix. Each interview is practice. Ask for feedback when you can.

Step 7: Land an internship or first role
Internships at product companies count as real experience on your resume. Even a stipend-based internship at a startup gives you real problems, real stakeholders, and something concrete to discuss in interviews.

Step 8: Keep growing on the job
Your first role is your real design school. Ship features, observe how users respond, and absorb how senior designers make decisions. Set a goal to run one full research cycle every quarter.

05 India Specific Tips

India Specific Tips

Where to study
Formal design degrees from NID (National Institute of Design) and the design programmes at IITs carry strong brand recognition with Indian recruiters. If a full-time degree is not an option, online courses plus a strong portfolio can substitute well in the startup ecosystem. Some candidates also do short intensive bootcamps offered by institutes in Bangalore and Mumbai.

Communities to join
India has active design communities worth joining early. The Design Community India on LinkedIn and Slack channels like 'Designers of India' are good places to share work and get feedback. Nasscom Design4India and local Adobe User Groups host events where you can meet working designers.

Job search reality on Naukri and LinkedIn
Naukri lists many 'UI/UX designer' roles that actually mean graphic design or Photoshop-only work. Read the job description carefully before applying. LinkedIn is stronger for product roles at funded startups. Filter by 'Product Designer' or 'UX Designer' and sort by date. Follow the companies you want to work at and engage with their posts before cold-applying.

Salary expectations
Salary data for product designers in India is publicly reported on platforms like Glassdoor and levels.fyi. Ranges vary widely by city, company stage, and your experience level. Check those platforms with a filter for your target city and company size to get a realistic picture.

Building visibility
Many Indian designers land their first role through LinkedIn posts sharing their portfolio or case studies. Writing a short post explaining a design decision you made, even on a practice project, builds credibility and often leads to direct recruiter messages.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

Do I need a design degree to become a product designer in India?

No. Many working product designers in India are self-taught or come from engineering, MBA, or communication backgrounds. What matters most to recruiters is your portfolio and your ability to explain your design decisions. A degree from NID or an IIT design programme helps with brand recognition, but it is not a gating requirement at most startups and mid-size tech companies.

How different is product design from UI/UX design?

The titles are often used interchangeably in Indian job postings, which creates confusion. In practice, 'product designer' usually signals a broader scope: you own the full problem from research to final visuals. 'UI designer' often means you focus mainly on visual execution. 'UX designer' often means research and flows. When you see a job posting, read the responsibilities section carefully rather than relying on the title alone.

What should my first portfolio project be about?

Pick a problem you have personally experienced or observed. Redesigning an existing app (a food delivery checkout flow, a banking app's onboarding) works well because recruiters instantly understand the before and after. What matters is showing your process: why you made specific decisions, what you tested, and what you learned. A single well-documented case study is worth more than several shallow ones.

Is Figma enough, or do I need to learn other tools too?

Figma covers the full design workflow for most product roles in India: wireframes, high-fidelity screens, prototypes, and developer handoff. You do not need to learn Adobe XD or Sketch separately to get your first job. Where Figma falls short is complex motion design, for which tools like After Effects or Rive are used, but that is a specialisation you can pick up later.

How do I handle 'no experience' when applying for junior roles?

Self-initiated projects and internships fill the gap effectively. A case study where you redesigned a broken app experience, even unprompted, shows the same skills as paid work. Recruiters at startups care more about whether you can think through a design problem than where you got the experience. Volunteering to redesign the site of a local NGO or a friend's small business and documenting it well counts as real work.

What is the career path after the first product designer role?

A common path in India goes from junior designer to product designer to senior designer to lead or principal designer. Some move into design management (design manager, head of design) while others go deep on craft as individual contributors. At funded startups, senior designers often get opportunities to build and lead small design teams, which opens the management track faster than at large enterprises.
