01 Overview

Overview

Roku sits at the crossroads of consumer hardware, streaming software, and digital advertising. Its business runs on multiple revenue lines: device sales, the Roku Channel (its free ad-supported streaming service), advertising on the Roku OS platform, and licensing fees from smart TV manufacturers. This makes Roku PM interviews unusually specific. You will be tested on your understanding of both the consumer experience and the platform's commercial model.

Candidates report that Roku interviews typically cover product sense, metrics, strategy, and behavioral rounds. The process often includes a take-home case or product teardown alongside live interviews. As of July 2026, Roku has 253 open roles tracked by knok jobradar, with active hiring across PM levels.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

Candidates report that Roku interviews typically include a mix of product sense, strategy, metrics, and behavioral questions. Here are the questions that come up most often:

  1. Walk me through the Roku product ecosystem and tell me how you would improve one part of it.
  2. How would you redesign the Roku home screen to increase content discovery and reduce the time it takes a user to start watching?
  3. Roku competes with Fire TV, Apple TV, and Google TV. How would you define Roku's differentiating product strategy for 2026-2027?
  4. How would you prioritize features for the Roku mobile app versus the on-screen TV experience?
  5. How would you design a new ad format for Roku that balances advertiser goals with a good viewer experience?
  6. A major content partner is threatening to pull their app from the Roku platform over revenue share terms. How do you handle this?
  7. Roku's data shows that many users browse for a long time without starting a show. What would you do?
  8. How would you measure the success of the Roku Channel?
  9. Tell me about a time you had to make a product decision with incomplete data. What was your process?
  10. How would you decide whether Roku should build, buy, or partner for a new AI-powered content recommendation engine?
  11. An A/B test shows a new UI increases session length but decreases the number of channels users browse. Which metric do you optimize for and why?
  12. Tell me about a product you shipped that required close collaboration with an advertising or monetization team.

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Q: How would you redesign the Roku home screen to increase content discovery?

*Situation:* In a previous role, I worked on a video-on-demand platform where users frequently opened the app, browsed for several minutes, and then closed it without watching anything.

*Task:* I was asked to lead a discovery feature redesign with a cross-functional team covering design, engineering, and data science.

*Action:* I started by mapping user journeys through session recordings and speaking with a small group of users. Three problems stood out: too many undifferentiated content tiles, no mood-based filtering, and a weak personalized 'continue watching' section. I worked with the data science team to build a 'For You' row based on watch history, collaborated with design to add a simple mood filter, and moved 'continue watching' to the top of the home screen. We ran a controlled test on a subset of users before rolling out widely.

*Result:* The test group showed a measurable improvement in time-to-first-play and overall session engagement. Leadership cited the project as a model for future discovery work.

---

Q: Tell me about a time you had to make a product decision with incomplete data.

*Situation:* We were planning a new onboarding flow for a mobile app. There was pressure to launch quickly, but we had only two weeks of beta data from a limited group of users.

*Task:* I needed to decide whether to proceed with the current design or delay the launch for more data.

*Action:* I identified the top three assumptions the design relied on and ran lightweight experiments to pressure-test each one within the available window. Where data was thin, I spoke directly with users who matched our target profile. I then built a simple risk matrix to show leadership the cost of shipping with a flawed assumption versus delaying by two weeks. We agreed to ship with one small change and set up a post-launch monitoring plan with clear rollback criteria.

*Result:* The launch went smoothly. Post-launch data confirmed the adjusted design held up, and the rollback plan was never triggered.

---

Q: A major content partner threatens to pull their app from the Roku platform. How do you handle this?

*Situation:* At a previous company, we ran a content marketplace and one of our top partners threatened to leave over concerns about our ranking algorithm deprioritizing their content.

*Task:* My role was to lead the internal response and work with the partner to reach a resolution without compromising our platform's fairness principles.

*Action:* I set up a call with the partner's product and business team to understand their specific concerns. I then worked with engineering to pull data on how their content had performed before and after our recent algorithm update. The data showed an unintended side effect from a legitimate change we had made. I proposed a short-term adjustment and a co-designed pilot to test a content promotion model that worked for both sides.

*Result:* The partner stayed on the platform. The pilot led to a new 'featured partner' feature that generated additional revenue for both sides, and the relationship improved significantly.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

STAR for behavioral questions. Structure every 'tell me about a time' answer with Situation, Task, Action, and Result. Keep the Situation and Task brief (two to three sentences each) and spend most of your time on Action and Result. Interviewers want to know what you actually did and what happened.

Jobs-to-be-Done for product sense. When asked to improve a Roku product, start with what job the user is trying to get done. For example: 'relax with familiar content after work' is a different job than 'discover a new show to binge.' Designing to serve a specific job produces sharper ideas than just listing features.

North Star Metric for metrics questions. Roku's likely North Star is something like total streaming hours or daily active streaming accounts. When asked about metrics, anchor your answer to a North Star and show how secondary metrics (time-to-first-play, ad completion rate, channel discovery rate) connect to it. Always explain what you would do if a secondary metric moves in an unexpected direction.

Two-sided platform framing for strategy questions. Roku connects viewers with content partners and advertisers. When asked about strategy or trade-offs, explicitly name both sides and show that you understand the tension. A decision that improves the viewer experience may reduce ad revenue. A decision that benefits a content partner may hurt discovery for users. Naming that tension and resolving it thoughtfully is what separates strong PM candidates.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Platform thinking. Roku is not just a consumer product. It is a platform connecting viewers with content partners and advertisers. Candidates who only think about the consumer side without considering how Roku makes money typically struggle in strategy and trade-off rounds.

Data fluency. Streaming generates enormous amounts of data. Interviewers want to see that you can define the right metrics, design a clean A/B test, and interpret results correctly. Vague answers like 'I would look at engagement' will not stand out.

Cross-functional leadership. Roku PMs work across engineering, design, content partnerships, ad sales, and legal. Show that you have navigated competing priorities across business units and can align teams without direct authority.

User empathy backed by research. Despite the business complexity, the best Roku PMs stay deeply user-focused. Interviewers want real examples of how you have used user research or qualitative feedback to change a product decision, not just 'I talked to users.'

Clear communication. Candidates report that Roku interviewers pay attention to how clearly you structure your thinking out loud. Practice thinking aloud in a structured way: state your goal, name your assumptions, work through your reasoning, and land on a clear recommendation.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Step 1: Learn the Roku business.

Use the Roku platform across devices if you can. Map out the major revenue streams: devices, advertising, the Roku Channel, and licensing. Read publicly available earnings call summaries or analyst notes to understand what Roku's leadership is prioritizing for 2025-2027.

Step 2: Practice core question types.

Focus on the four question types you are most likely to face: product improvement, metrics, strategy, and behavioral. Write out two full STAR stories covering cross-functional leadership and data-driven decision making. Practice them aloud so they feel natural, not rehearsed.

Step 3: Build a Roku product teardown.

Pick one Roku feature (the home screen, the Roku Channel, or the ad experience) and prepare a short product teardown. Cover what the feature does well, what you would improve, and how you would measure success. Be ready to present this in five minutes if asked.

Step 4: Run mock interviews.

Do at least two full mock interviews with a peer or a PM interview coach. Use the frameworks from this guide. After each mock, write down the two weakest moments and work on those specifically.

Step 5: Calibrate your salary expectations.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Treating Roku like a pure consumer product. Many candidates forget that Roku's revenue comes largely from advertising and platform fees. Product improvement ideas that ignore monetization will not land well in strategy rounds.

Giving generic metrics. Saying 'I would track engagement and retention' is not enough. Name the specific metric, explain how you would measure it, and connect it to a business outcome.

Skipping the trade-off conversation. Roku operates a two-sided platform. Almost every product decision involves a tension between the viewer experience and what content partners or advertisers want. Show that you see both sides and can resolve the tension with a clear rationale.

Weak STAR answers. Candidates often spend too long on the Situation and rush through the Result. Interviewers want to know what actually happened. If you shipped something, what moved? If you made a call under pressure, what was the outcome?

Not knowing the product. It is surprisingly common for candidates to have never used Roku or to be unfamiliar with the Roku Channel. Spend real time on the platform before your interview. Generic answers that could apply to any streaming service will not stand out.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

How many rounds does a Roku PM interview typically have?

Candidates report that Roku PM interviews typically run across four to six rounds. These commonly include a recruiter screen, a hiring manager conversation, a product sense round, a metrics or analytical round, a behavioral round, and sometimes a take-home exercise or presentation. Round count and structure vary by level and team, so ask your recruiter for specifics when you get the call.

What salary can I expect for a PM role at Roku if I am based in India?

Most Roku PM roles are based in the US. For India-market context, knok jobradar data shows PM salaries at 24-40 LPA for the 3-6 year experience band and 40-60 LPA at the senior level. For US-based Roku roles, levels.fyi carries publicly reported compensation data specific to the company and level. Check there for the most current figures before any offer conversation.

Does Roku give a take-home case for PM interviews?

Candidates report that Roku sometimes assigns a take-home product case or asks for a product teardown presentation, particularly at the mid to senior level. Typically this involves proposing improvements to a Roku product and presenting your reasoning to a panel. Not all teams use this format, so confirm with your recruiter early in the process.

How important is advertising knowledge for a Roku PM role?

It depends on the team. Roku has both consumer-focused PM roles (home screen, search, device OS) and advertising or monetization PM roles (ad products, the Roku Channel, programmatic). For advertising PM roles, knowledge of ad tech basics like targeting, measurement, and CPM models is important. For consumer roles, understanding how advertising fits into Roku's business model is still useful context.

How should I prepare for the metrics round at Roku?

Practice defining a North Star metric and connecting secondary metrics to it. For streaming products, commonly cited metrics include daily active viewers, average streaming hours per user, time-to-first-play, and ad completion rate. Be ready to explain how you would design an A/B test, what your success criteria would be, and how you would respond to unexpected results. Interviewers want to see structured thinking, not just a list of metrics.

Are there Roku PM openings I can apply to from India?

Roku is a US-headquartered company and most PM roles are based in the US. Knok jobradar currently shows 253 open roles at Roku across functions. Indian candidates typically target positions that allow remote work or are open to relocation. Always check the specific job listing for location requirements, as policies vary by team and role.
