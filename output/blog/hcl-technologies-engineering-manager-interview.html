01 Overview

Overview

HCL Technologies is one of India's largest IT services companies, with 349 open roles on knok jobradar as of July 2026. Engineering Manager positions span delivery management, people leadership, and direct client engagement, so interviews typically test all three areas together.

The hiring process candidates report usually runs across three to five rounds: an initial HR screen, one or two rounds with senior engineering or delivery leaders, a leadership and behavioural interview, and sometimes a final conversation with a business unit head. Timelines vary by division, but candidates typically hear back within two to four weeks of the first round.

HCL places particular weight on large-scale delivery experience, since many of its Engineering Manager roles sit at the intersection of client management and engineering execution. Familiarity with HCL's service lines (engineering and R&D services, digital business, cloud, and AI) helps you tailor answers to the specific business context of the team you are joining.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

These questions come up repeatedly in Engineering Manager interviews at HCL, based on what candidates publicly report.

  1. Walk me through a large, multi-team programme you delivered end to end. What did you personally own and what was the outcome?
  2. How do you handle a situation where a key client is escalating on delivery timelines but your team is already stretched thin?
  3. Describe how you build and retain strong engineering talent in an IT services environment where attrition is high.
  4. How would you align your team's engineering roadmap with a client's business goals rather than just their stated technical requirements?
  5. Tell me about a time you had to turn around an underperforming team or project. What steps did you take and what changed?
  6. How do you manage competing priorities when you have multiple stakeholders, each believing their request is most urgent?
  7. Describe your approach to performance management. How do you address a consistently low performer while keeping team morale intact?
  8. What is your experience with offshore-onsite delivery models, and how do you manage collaboration across time zones?
  9. HCL is investing heavily in AI and automation. How have you led or supported an automation initiative that changed how your team works?
  10. How do you handle scope creep from a client who keeps adding requirements without adjusting timelines or budgets?
  11. Describe a situation where you disagreed with a senior stakeholder's technical or delivery decision. How did you handle it?
  12. What metrics do you track to know whether an engineering team is healthy and delivering well?

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Use the STAR format for behavioural questions. Keep Situation and Task concise, a sentence or two each, then spend the bulk of your answer on what you specifically did and what changed as a result.

Q: Tell me about a time you turned around an underperforming team.

*Situation:* I was brought in to lead a team of twelve engineers that had missed its last three sprint commitments on a banking client's digital transformation project.

*Task:* My goal was to stabilise delivery within one quarter while understanding the root causes so the same pattern would not repeat.

*Action:* I spent the first two weeks doing one-on-ones with every team member and listening to the client account manager. I found two core issues: unclear module ownership, and a testing bottleneck that sat entirely with one overloaded senior engineer. I reorganised ownership so each engineer had a named component, paired the senior engineer with two others to spread testing knowledge, and introduced a brief daily sync focused only on blockers. I also had a candid conversation with the client to reset expectations on one feature that was genuinely under-scoped.

*Result:* The team hit its commitments for two consecutive sprints by the end of that quarter. The client renewed the statement of work for a follow-on phase, and one engineer who had been flagged for a performance improvement plan was promoted to tech lead the following year.

---

Q: How have you led an automation initiative that changed how your team works?

*Situation:* Our QA process relied on manual regression testing before every release, which was creating a bottleneck and pushing release cycles out by several days.

*Task:* I needed to introduce automated testing without disrupting an active delivery schedule or asking the team to absorb learning as extra work on top of their sprints.

*Action:* I identified two engineers who were already curious about test automation and gave them dedicated sprint time to build a foundational test suite using the framework the client's own QA team preferred. I made automation coverage a tracked metric in sprint reviews so it got visibility. I also brought in a short internal workshop from HCL's engineering excellence community to upskill the broader team.

*Result:* Within two quarters, automated tests covered the core regression paths. Release preparation time dropped noticeably, freeing the team to take on a new feature track the client had been waiting to prioritise.

---

Q: Describe how you manage competing priorities from multiple stakeholders.

*Situation:* I was managing a team supporting two internal product owners and one external client, all of whom believed their backlog items were critical.

*Task:* I needed a fair, transparent way to prioritise that would not feel arbitrary to any stakeholder.

*Action:* I introduced a simple scoring exercise where each stakeholder rated their top items on business impact and time sensitivity. I ran a monthly prioritisation call where all parties could see the full backlog and the scoring, not just their own slice. I was transparent about capacity so stakeholders could make trade-offs themselves rather than always escalating to me. When items were genuinely tied, I escalated with a clear written recommendation and a stated trade-off, not just the problem.

*Result:* Escalations to senior leadership dropped over the following two quarters. More importantly, stakeholders told me they felt heard even when their item was not picked first, because the reasoning was visible.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) is the expected format for behavioural questions. Keep Situation and Task brief, then spend most of your time on what you specifically did and what changed as a result. Avoid 'we' throughout; interviewers want to hear what you personally owned.

The Delivery Lens works well for HCL-specific questions. When asked about any challenge, frame it across three layers: the client relationship (what the client needed and how you managed expectations), the team (how you organised and supported the people), and the process (what you changed or introduced to make delivery reliable). This mirrors how HCL Engineering Managers actually operate.

The Numbers Trap is worth avoiding. Many candidates inflate or fabricate metrics. If you do not have a precise figure, say 'noticeably' or 'significantly' and explain qualitatively. A confident 'the client extended the contract for a follow-on phase' is stronger than an invented percentage.

Handling Hypothetical Questions like 'what would you do if...' are best answered with a short real example followed by a principle: 'In a similar situation I did X, and the principle I would apply here is Y.' This shows both experience and clear thinking.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

HCL Engineering Manager interviews typically look for a specific combination of qualities.

Client orientation. HCL is an IT services company, so the client relationship is central. Interviewers want evidence that you can manage client expectations through difficult conversations, not just deliver when things go smoothly.

Delivery at scale. The role typically involves coordinating across multiple sub-teams, vendors, and geographies. Concrete examples of managing large, distributed programmes carry more weight than examples from small, co-located teams.

People leadership depth. Candidates who can describe specific instances of coaching, performance management, and career development for their engineers stand out. Generic statements about 'building a culture of ownership' without examples do not land well.

Business and commercial awareness. Understanding revenue impact, contract structures, or how engineering decisions affect margins is a differentiator at this level. You do not need deep financial expertise, but you should be able to speak to how your delivery decisions affected the business case.

Composure under pressure. HCL interviewers often probe how you respond when things go wrong. Honest answers that include mistakes and what you learned are received better than perfectly polished stories with no setbacks.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Two to three weeks out: Research HCL's current focus areas, particularly engineering and R&D services, cloud, and AI initiatives, which are publicly reported as strategic priorities. Review the specific job description carefully and map your experience to the stated responsibilities.

One to two weeks out: Prepare at least six to eight STAR stories covering: a delivery turnaround, a difficult client conversation, a talent challenge, a cross-team conflict, an automation or process improvement, and a situation where you disagreed with a stakeholder. Write these out so you can recall them clearly under pressure.

Three to five days out: Practice saying your answers aloud. Record yourself if you can. Notice filler words and whether you are saying 'we' when you should be saying 'I'. Ask a peer to challenge your answers with follow-up questions.

Day before: Prepare two or three genuine questions for your interviewers. Good ones focus on what success looks like in the first six months, what the biggest delivery challenge the team currently faces, and how Engineering Managers at HCL typically collaborate with the client-side team.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Talking about the team instead of yourself. 'We delivered' tells the interviewer nothing about your contribution. Be specific about what you personally owned, decided, or changed.

Under-preparing for client management questions. Many Engineering Manager candidates practise only technical and people-management questions. At HCL, client-facing scenarios carry equal weight and catch many candidates off guard.

Giving vague answers about impact. Saying 'I improved team performance' without any qualifier feels hollow. Describe what changed in observable terms: sprint predictability improved, escalations dropped, the client signed a renewal.

Not researching the business unit. HCL has multiple divisions. An Engineering Manager role in engineering and R&D services is different from one in digital business or cloud. Tailoring at least one answer to the specific division shows genuine interest.

Overly long answers. Candidates report that HCL interviewers move at a steady pace across several questions. An answer that runs much longer than two to three minutes on a single question can leave you without time to show range across other topics.

Ignoring the offshore-onsite dimension. HCL's delivery model is built on distributed teams. If you have not addressed collaboration across time zones somewhere in your answers, you have missed a core expectation of the role.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

How many rounds does the HCL Engineering Manager interview typically have?

Candidates typically report three to five rounds for Engineering Manager roles at HCL. This usually includes an HR screening call, one or two rounds with senior engineering or delivery leaders, a leadership and behavioural interview, and sometimes a final conversation with a business unit head. Exact structures vary by division and location, so confirm the process with your recruiter after the first round.

What salary can I expect as an Engineering Manager at HCL?

Based on knok jobradar data, Engineering Manager roles sit in a band of 35-60 LPA, Senior Manager roles in the 55-90 LPA range, and Director-level roles at 90-150+ LPA. These are indicative ranges from job listings and may vary based on the specific business unit, your total experience, and the city of posting. Bangalore, which has the most open roles among the cities tracked, may carry a different cost-of-living premium compared to other locations.

Does HCL ask coding or technical architecture questions in Engineering Manager interviews?

At the Engineering Manager level, candidates report that deep coding tests are rare. Interviewers typically focus on your ability to evaluate technical decisions, guide architecture discussions, and support your team rather than write code yourself. You should be comfortable discussing system design trade-offs and technology choices relevant to your domain, since HCL interviewers may probe how you handle technical disagreements within your team or with the client.

How important is HCL-specific knowledge going into the interview?

Knowing HCL's service lines and strategic direction, which are publicly reported in their annual communications, helps you contextualise answers and signals genuine interest. Understanding the offshore-onsite delivery model is particularly important since it is core to how HCL operates. You do not need to memorise HCL's financials, but being able to speak to how your experience maps to their business model (IT services, engineering R&D, digital transformation) is a clear differentiator.

Where are most HCL Engineering Manager roles located?

According to knok jobradar data as of July 2026, Bangalore has the highest concentration of Engineering Manager openings among the cities tracked, followed by Delhi. Pune, Chennai, Hyderabad, and Mumbai also have openings but at lower volumes. If you are open to relocation, Bangalore gives you the widest choice of roles across HCL's different divisions.

What is the best way to follow up after an HCL interview?

Candidates typically send a brief follow-up note to the HR contact within a day of each round, thanking the interviewers and reiterating interest. Keep it short and genuine. If you do not hear back within the timeframe the recruiter quoted, one polite follow-up is appropriate. Avoid following up multiple times in quick succession, as HCL's hiring timelines can stretch depending on internal approvals and headcount release cycles.
