01 Overview

Overview

Airwallex is a global fintech company building financial infrastructure for businesses: cross-border payments, multi-currency accounts, expense management, and embedded finance APIs. Their Product Design team works on B2B products used by startups and enterprises across Asia-Pacific, Europe, and the US. The role demands strong systems thinking, comfort with dense financial data, and the ability to simplify compliance-heavy flows without losing clarity for the end user.

As of July 2026, Airwallex has 610 open roles across its global offices, signalling significant hiring activity. Interview processes typically span a recruiter screen, a portfolio review, a design exercise, and cross-functional interviews with PMs and engineers. Candidates report that Airwallex panels place high value on how you articulate trade-offs and handle ambiguity in regulated, multi-market financial products.

02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

These are the questions candidates report most often in Airwallex Product Designer interviews. Expect a mix of portfolio deep-dives, process questions, and situational prompts.

  1. Walk us through a complex product you designed end-to-end. What was the biggest design challenge you faced?
  2. Airwallex serves businesses across many countries and regulations. How have you designed for users with different regulatory or cultural contexts?
  3. Tell us about a time you simplified a complex, multi-step flow (such as onboarding or payment initiation). What was your process?
  4. How do you collaborate with engineers when a design you proposed is technically difficult to build on the given timeline?
  5. Describe a project where business goals and user needs were in conflict. How did you decide what to prioritise?
  6. How do you approach designing data-heavy dashboards or financial reporting screens where information density is unavoidable?
  7. Give an example of receiving conflicting feedback from multiple stakeholders. How did you resolve it?
  8. How do you validate your designs when direct user research access is limited or timelines are very tight?
  9. Walk us through how you would redesign the onboarding experience for a new business signing up on Airwallex for the first time.
  10. How do you factor in accessibility and internationalisation when designing for a global product?
  11. Tell us about a design decision that did not work out as expected. What did you learn from it?
  12. How do you stay current on fintech design trends, and how do you decide which trends are actually worth applying to your work?

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Q: Tell us about a time you simplified a complex, multi-step flow.

*Situation:* I was designing the onboarding flow for a B2B SaaS platform that required KYC document collection, business verification, and account configuration, all in a single session.

*Task:* The existing flow had many screens with no progress indication, and the product team noted that completion rates were low.

*Action:* I mapped every step a new user had to take and grouped related actions into three logical stages: identity, business details, and setup. I introduced a persistent progress bar and added a 'save and return later' option for steps requiring documents. I ran unmoderated usability tests with a small group of participants using a prototype and iterated on the design based on their feedback.

*Result:* After launch, the product team reported a meaningful improvement in onboarding completion. The 'save and return' feature became the most-used element in the first two weeks, validating the assumption that document collection was the main drop-off point.

---

Q: How do you collaborate with engineers when a design is technically difficult to build?

*Situation:* On a dashboard project, I had designed an animated real-time chart update that the front-end team said would cause performance issues on older browsers.

*Task:* I needed to find a solution that preserved the clarity of the real-time data without degrading the experience for a significant portion of users.

*Action:* I scheduled a short working session with the lead engineer to understand the constraint. Together we explored a polling approach with a manual refresh action rather than a live animation. I revised the design to make the refresh action prominent and reassuring, so users felt in control of when data updated.

*Result:* The revised design shipped on time. User feedback on the dashboard was positive, and the manual refresh pattern was later adopted as a standard across other data panels in the product.

---

Q: Describe a project where business goals and user needs were in conflict.

*Situation:* A stakeholder wanted to add a prominent upsell banner inside the main transaction screen to drive upgrade conversions.

*Task:* My concern was that the banner would interrupt users during a high-focus, time-sensitive task (initiating a payment), potentially causing errors or frustration.

*Action:* I proposed an A/B test framing to the stakeholder: we could test a contextual upsell shown only after a successful transaction (a natural pause moment) against the in-flow banner. I created both variants and presented the rationale clearly, tying it to task completion rate as a shared metric both design and business cared about.

*Result:* The stakeholder agreed to run the test. The post-transaction variant achieved a higher click-through rate without negatively affecting task completion, giving the business the conversion lift it wanted without disrupting the core user journey.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

Portfolio presentation: For every case study, structure it as: the problem (what the business and user needed), your process (research, ideation, testing), the key trade-off you made and why, and the outcome. Airwallex panels typically probe the 'why' behind decisions more than the aesthetics of the final design.

Design exercise: Candidates report receiving take-home exercises or whiteboard prompts around fintech flows such as onboarding, payment initiation, or expense approval. Use a simple structure: clarify constraints, state your assumptions, map the user journey, identify the single biggest friction point, then design to resolve it. Talk through your reasoning at every step.

Trade-off questions: When asked how you balance competing priorities (speed vs. polish, user preference vs. compliance requirement), name the constraint clearly, explain what you chose to optimise for and why it served the most important goal at that moment, and describe what you would revisit with more time or resources.

Failure questions: These test self-awareness, not a trap. Choose a real example, describe what specifically went wrong (not vague 'learnings'), and explain how you changed your process afterward. Panels respond well to concrete, specific answers here.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Airwallex Product Designer interviews, based on what candidates report, look for a few specific qualities beyond strong visual craft.

Systems thinking over single-screen polish. Airwallex products are interconnected: a payment flow touches notifications, compliance checks, currency conversion, and account states. Interviewers want to see that you think about how a design decision in one area affects adjacent flows.

Comfort with constraint. Financial products have hard constraints: regulatory requirements, API limitations, and strict data formats. Interviewers look for designers who treat these as design inputs rather than blockers, and who can create clarity within tight boundaries.

Clear articulation of trade-offs. 'I chose this because' should appear often in your answers. Panels are less interested in whether you made the right call and more interested in whether your reasoning is structured and honest.

Cross-functional fluency. You will be asked how you work with PMs and engineers. Show that you treat technical and product feedback as data for the design, not as criticism to defend against.

User empathy grounded in evidence. Saying 'users will find it confusing' is weaker than 'in our usability test, most participants missed the secondary action.' Bring qualitative or quantitative data to support your design judgements.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Two weeks before the interview

Spend time inside the Airwallex product if you can access the demo or public-facing flows. Pay attention to how they handle onboarding, dashboard layouts, and multi-step transactions. Read their public blog and any design or engineering posts to understand how they talk about their product philosophy.

Review your portfolio and select two or three case studies most relevant to B2B fintech: flows with real complexity, research you ran, and clear outcomes. Prepare to go deep on each one, because interviewers will ask follow-up questions well beyond what is on the slide.

One week before

Practise answering the 12 questions listed in this guide out loud, not just in your head. Record yourself if possible. Pay attention to whether your answers are specific (names, situations, outcomes) or vague (general statements about process).

Prepare questions to ask the panel. Strong ones for Airwallex: How does the design team collaborate with the compliance and regulatory team when building new product flows? How is design success measured within the product organisation?

Day before

Review Airwallex's recent product announcements and news. Check their open roles page to understand which teams are growing. This context helps you connect your experience to where the company is heading, a signal interviewers notice.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Presenting work without explaining decisions. Showing polished screens without walking through why you made specific choices is the most common mistake. Interviewers at product-led companies like Airwallex want to understand your thinking, not just your output.

Staying at the surface of complex flows. When asked to redesign an onboarding or payment experience, candidates often jump to UI solutions without first mapping the full user journey or naming the core problem they are solving. Start with the problem.

Vague answers to trade-off questions. 'I balanced user needs with business goals' tells an interviewer nothing. Name the specific tension, the specific choice, and the specific reason you made it.

Not asking clarifying questions in the design exercise. Candidates who assume scope tend to solve the wrong problem. Ask what success looks like, who the primary user is, and what constraints exist before you start designing.

Ignoring the B2B context. Airwallex's users are business operators and finance teams, not casual consumers. Designs that prioritise consumer-style simplicity over the information density business users need can signal a mismatch in understanding the user base.

Skipping the outcome. Every case study should end with what happened after launch: adoption, qualitative feedback, what you would do differently. Omitting this makes your work look unfinished.

What portfolio case studies should I focus on for an Airwallex interview?

Prioritise case studies that show you can handle complexity: multi-step flows, data-heavy dashboards, or products with hard constraints such as compliance or technical limitations.
