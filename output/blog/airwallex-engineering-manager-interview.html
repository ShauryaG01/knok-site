02 Most Asked Questions

Most Asked Questions

Airwallex interviews for Engineering Manager roles typically test leadership depth, technical fluency, and fintech awareness. Here are the questions candidates most commonly report:

  1. Walk me through how you led a team through a major product launch, from planning to post-launch retrospective.
  2. How do you identify and support an underperforming engineer? Give a specific example.
  3. Airwallex operates in a regulated payments space. How do you balance engineering speed with compliance and reliability?
  4. Describe your experience with distributed systems or real-time transaction processing, and how it shapes the way you lead your team.
  5. How do you set team priorities when you have competing requests from multiple product managers?
  6. Tell me about a technical trade-off you made under time pressure. What was the outcome, and what would you do differently?
  7. How have you scaled an engineering team, including hiring, onboarding, and culture-setting?
  8. How do you handle strong disagreements between senior engineers on an architectural decision?
  9. Give an example where you drove a product or process change without having direct authority over all stakeholders.
  10. How do you manage technical debt while keeping your team's delivery velocity high?
  11. Describe how you have built a culture of ownership and accountability on your team.
  12. What does good engineering look like in a fintech company, and how do you measure it?

03 Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Sample Answers (STAR Format)

Q: How do you handle an underperforming engineer?

*Situation:* I had a senior engineer whose output had dropped noticeably over two quarters. Peers were quietly compensating, which was creating tension across the team.

*Task:* My job was to understand the root cause, support the engineer, and protect the team without losing someone who held deep institutional knowledge.

*Action:* I set up weekly one-on-ones focused on removing blockers, not status updates. I asked directly: 'What is making this feel hard right now?' It turned out the engineer felt their work was invisible to leadership and was unclear about career progression. I co-created a 60-day plan: leading a design review, writing an RFC, and presenting at a team demo. I also set clear, measurable expectations in writing so there was no ambiguity.

*Result:* Within two months the engineer's output improved noticeably and they led the RFC process for a new data pipeline. They were promoted the following cycle. The team saw that I addressed problems rather than letting them fester.

---

Q: How do you balance engineering speed with compliance in a regulated environment?

*Situation:* Our team needed to ship a new payment routing feature in six weeks to meet a partner commitment. Legal flagged that the feature touched fund-flow logic requiring a compliance review, which typically takes four weeks.

*Task:* I had to meet the deadline without bypassing compliance or burning the team out trying to do both in parallel.

*Action:* I ran a joint session with legal, compliance, and engineering in week one to map exactly which parts of the code touched regulated paths. We restructured the feature so the non-regulated routing logic shipped in week three, with the regulated piece sitting behind a feature flag. Compliance reviewed only the scoped portion, cutting their review time to two weeks. I kept the partner updated with a phased rollout plan they accepted.

*Result:* The core feature shipped on time. The regulated path followed one week later with full sign-off. No compliance incidents, and the partner renewed the contract.

---

Q: How have you driven change without direct authority?

*Situation:* Our platform team and a product squad had conflicting views on API versioning. The product team wanted to deprecate old versions quickly; the platform team wanted a long support window. I led the platform team and had no authority over the product squad.

*Task:* I needed a workable agreement that did not slow product velocity or leave my team maintaining a fragmented API surface indefinitely.

*Action:* I put together a one-page cost analysis showing the engineering hours spent maintaining each deprecated version over the previous year. I shared it with both engineering managers and the respective product managers in a joint session. I proposed a six-month deprecation window with automated migration tooling that my team would own, removing the burden from the product team. Both sides co-wrote the final policy, giving each team a sense of ownership.

*Result:* Both teams adopted the policy. We deprecated four old API versions over the following two quarters. Platform maintenance hours for those versions dropped, freeing engineers for new feature work.

04 Answer Frameworks

Answer Frameworks

STAR for behavioural questions. Structure every answer as Situation, Task, Action, Result. Keep the Situation brief (two to three sentences). Spend most of your time on Action: what you specifically did, the choices you made, and why. End with a concrete Result, preferably something observable or measurable.

The Leadership Compass for people questions. When asked about managing someone, cover four dimensions: clarity (did the person know what was expected?), capability (did they have the skills, or did they need development?), motivation (what was driving or blocking them?), and support (what did you remove from their path?). This signals systematic thinking rather than instinct-based management.

Trade-off framing for technical and process questions. Airwallex interviewers reportedly appreciate when candidates frame decisions as trade-offs rather than right-or-wrong choices. Name what you optimised for (speed, reliability, team health, compliance), what you gave up, and what signal would tell you to revisit the decision.

Influence without authority. For cross-functional scenarios, show three things: that you understood the other team's incentives, that you brought data or shared cost visibility, and that you made collaboration easier rather than demanding compliance.

05 What Interviewers Want

What Interviewers Want

Technical credibility without micromanagement. You do not need to write production code daily, but you must be able to review an architecture diagram, spot risks in a technical plan, and hold a genuine conversation with a staff engineer. Candidates who cannot engage technically are typically filtered out early.

Fintech awareness. Interviewers want to see that you understand why reliability, security, and regulatory compliance are non-negotiable in payments. Prior fintech experience is not required, but you should show that you have thought about what changes when real money is on the line.

People-first outcomes. Rapid scaling means Airwallex values managers who can hire well, onboard fast, and retain engineers in a competitive market. Stories about engineers you grew or helped get promoted tend to land well.

Bias toward action. The company moves quickly. Candidates who show they can make decisions with incomplete information, communicate the reasoning clearly, and adjust when new data arrives typically score higher than those who emphasise approval chains and process gates.

Cross-functional fluency. Engineering Managers at Airwallex work closely with Product, Design, Legal, and Finance. Showing that you can build trust and influence outcomes outside your team is important.

06 Preparation Plan

Preparation Plan

Week 1: Know the company. Read Airwallex's publicly available engineering blog posts and recent product announcements. Understand their core products: global accounts, payment gateway, and FX infrastructure. Know what problems their business customers are trying to solve. Prepare a specific two-to-three sentence answer to 'Why Airwallex?' that goes beyond 'fast-growing fintech.'

Week 2: Build your story bank. Write out eight to ten real examples from your career covering: a team you scaled, a conflict you resolved, a hard technical decision, a time you failed and recovered, and a time you influenced without authority. Map each to the STAR format. Practice saying them out loud until the timeline is clean and the result is specific.

Week 3: Technical refresh. Review distributed systems concepts relevant to payments: consistency models, idempotency, retry logic, and event-driven architecture. You will not need to write code, but you should discuss trade-offs at a design level. Prepare one or two examples where your team's technical choices were driven by reliability or correctness requirements.

Before each round. Prepare two to three thoughtful questions for your interviewer. Focus on team topology, how engineering priorities are set, and what success looks like in the first six months. Avoid asking about things easily found on the company website.

07 Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes

Being vague about results. 'The project went well' tells the interviewer nothing. Describe what changed as a result of your actions: the feature launched on time, team attrition dropped, the incident rate fell. You do not need a precise number if a clear observable shift is present.

Skipping the 'why'. Many candidates describe what they did but not why they chose that approach over alternatives. Airwallex interviewers, candidates report, probe for reasoning. Name the trade-off you were making every time.

Over-indexing on process. Talking about frameworks and ceremonies without showing judgment about when to skip or adapt them signals rigidity. Show that you adapt to context rather than following a playbook.

Underselling people development. Engineering Manager interviews are as much about growing engineers as delivering features. If you have not prepared stories about someone you coached through a struggle or helped get promoted, add them before your interview.

Not asking questions. Candidates who ask nothing, or only generic questions, signal low interest. Prepare specific questions about the team, the tech stack, or the product challenge you would be joining.

Confusing 'fintech experience' with 'fintech awareness'. You do not need a payments background. What matters is showing that you understand what is different about building software where money moves: regulatory constraints, error costs, and reliability standards.

Frequently asked

How many rounds does the Airwallex Engineering Manager interview typically have?

Candidates typically report four to six rounds. These commonly include a recruiter screen, a hiring manager conversation, one or two behavioural rounds with senior leaders, a technical or system design discussion, and a final culture or values round. Round names and sequence can vary, so confirm the process with your recruiter after the first call.

Do I need fintech or payments experience to get an Engineering Manager role at Airwallex?

Not necessarily. Airwallex has hired Engineering Managers from e-commerce, SaaS, and infrastructure backgrounds. What matters more is showing that you understand the implications of building software where money moves: reliability requirements, compliance considerations, and the cost of production errors. Prepare to discuss how your past work required similar rigour, even if the domain was different.

How should I answer 'Why Airwallex?' in the interview?

Be specific rather than generic. Reference something real: their cross-border payment infrastructure, a product line relevant to your experience, or the engineering scale of the problems they solve. A strong answer connects Airwallex's mission to something you have cared about in your past work. Avoid saying things like 'great culture' or 'growing fast' without backing them up with a concrete detail.

How do I prepare for the technical portion of an Engineering Manager interview at Airwallex?

You are unlikely to write code, but you may be asked to discuss system design at a high level, particularly around distributed systems, consistency models, or API design. Review concepts like idempotency, event-driven architecture, and trade-offs between consistency and availability. More importantly, prepare to talk about how you have guided your team through technical decisions, not just what those decisions were.
