01 Hiring Overview

Hiring Overview

Cursor is the company behind one of the most widely discussed AI coding tools in recent years. Their editor, built on VS Code, lets developers write, edit, and debug code with AI assistance, and it has built a strong following among software engineers globally. As of July 2026, Cursor has 119 open roles spanning engineering, machine learning, product, design, go-to-market, and operations.

Most roles are listed for US locations or are remote-friendly, but Cursor has been expanding its global team. Indian engineers with backgrounds in machine learning, large language models, full-stack development, or developer tooling are strong candidates, especially for roles that can be done remotely across compatible timezones.

The company is in a high-growth phase. They move quickly, expect candidates who can ship without hand-holding, and value people who genuinely care about making developers more productive. If you use Cursor every day and have strong opinions about what makes a coding tool great, that is already a meaningful advantage when you apply.

03 Interview Process

Interview Process

Publicly reported candidate accounts suggest the Cursor interview process has four to five stages, though the exact steps vary by team and seniority. Treat this as a rough guide, not a fixed script.

Application and initial screen: A recruiter or hiring manager reviews your resume and any public work you link. For engineering roles, an active GitHub profile or contributions to developer tools gives you a clear edge. A short note explaining how you actually use Cursor in your own work can help your application get noticed among many submissions.

Recruiter or hiring manager call: A short conversation about your background, your reasons for wanting to join, and basic role fit. Be ready to talk concretely about projects you have shipped and what impact they had on real users or systems.

Technical screen: For engineering candidates, this is typically a live coding session or a take-home assignment. Based on publicly reported experiences, Cursor tends to focus on problem-solving and code quality rather than purely algorithmic puzzles, though strong fundamentals in data structures and complexity are still expected.

System design or domain round: Senior candidates usually face a design interview. ML and AI applicants may be asked to discuss model architectures, fine-tuning strategies, or inference performance. Full-stack candidates typically cover API design, caching, and system scalability under load.

Final round: A set of conversations with engineering leads and sometimes members of the founding team. Expect questions about how you make decisions under ambiguity, how you think about product quality from a user's perspective, and how you collaborate with others. Intellectual curiosity and genuine passion for developer tools come through clearly at this stage.

04 What They Look For

What They Look For

Cursor is a product-led company that cares deeply about craft. Here is what consistently comes up across their job descriptions and publicly reported interviews.

Strong engineering fundamentals. Whether you are applying for a backend, ML, or full-stack role, Cursor expects you to understand systems at depth. Shallow knowledge covered by frameworks is straightforward to spot in their technical rounds.

AI and LLM fluency. Given what Cursor builds, familiarity with how large language models work, how to prompt them effectively, and how to integrate them into real products is a meaningful plus for almost every technical role. You do not need to have trained a model from scratch, but you should understand inference, context windows, tokenization, and basic evaluation approaches.

Shipping velocity. Cursor values engineers who can move from idea to working product quickly. If your portfolio shows projects that went from zero to real users, that signals the kind of ownership they want on their team.

Product sensibility. Cursor engineers are expected to think about the user, not just the code. If you have filed a detailed bug report, contributed to a product discussion, or redesigned something because it felt broken, mention it clearly in your interviews.

Clear written communication. The team is distributed and fast-moving. A lot of coordination happens asynchronously, so the ability to explain your thinking concisely in writing is treated as a core skill, not a soft one.

05 How To Prepare

How To Prepare

Use the product deeply before you apply. This sounds obvious, but many candidates skip it. Spend real time in Cursor. Learn its keyboard shortcuts, its AI features, and its edge cases. Form genuine opinions about what it does well and where it could improve. You will almost certainly be asked about your experience with the tool.

Build something with or for Cursor. A small project that showcases your ability to use or extend AI coding tools is far more persuasive than a polished resume alone. Even a weekend project with a public repo and a brief write-up of what you learned goes a long way in demonstrating the kind of initiative Cursor looks for.

Review LLM fundamentals. For any technical role at Cursor, you should be comfortable discussing how transformers work at a conceptual level, what retrieval-augmented generation is, and how prompt engineering affects model output quality. You do not need research-level depth, but you should be able to hold a substantive technical conversation.

Prepare concrete project stories. Use the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result) for at least three or four past projects. Tie results to real outcomes: user growth, latency improvements, or revenue impact from your own project data.

Practice system design for developer tools. Think through how you would design a code completion service, an AI chat interface with long context, or a code indexing pipeline. These scenarios map directly to what Cursor builds and show you have thought about their problem space.

Prepare questions that show depth. Ask about how they evaluate model quality, how they balance latency with accuracy, or how they think about code privacy. These questions signal you are thinking like an engineer with real curiosity, not just someone going through interview motions.

06 Culture And Values

Culture And Values

Cursor is a small, intense team working at the intersection of AI and software development. A few things stand out from what is publicly known about working there.

High ownership. People at Cursor are expected to own problems end to end. If you find a bug, you fix it. If you see a product gap, you propose a solution. The culture rewards initiative and is not a good fit for people who prefer clear-cut task lists handed down by managers.

Obsession with developer experience. The team uses their own product constantly. There is a strong internal culture of 'eating your own cooking,' meaning feedback loops are tight and quality expectations are high because everyone on the team is also a daily user of the product.

Speed as a value. Cursor has grown very fast and the internal pace reflects that. Decisions get made quickly, shipped, and iterated on. If you thrive in structured, slow-moving environments, Cursor is probably not the right fit for you.

Intellectual honesty. Publicly reported accounts from employees point to a culture where disagreement is expected to be voiced with reasoning, not suppressed. Being wrong is acceptable. Being unclear is not.

For Indian candidates specifically, the remote culture means you can contribute meaningfully from India, though you may need to overlap with US Pacific Time for some synchronous meetings. This is worth clarifying directly during your recruiter call so there are no surprises later.

Q Questions

Frequently asked

Does Cursor hire engineers based in India?

Cursor lists many roles as remote-friendly, and publicly reported employee profiles include people working from multiple countries. Indian candidates have applied and been hired into technical roles, particularly in engineering and ML. The best approach is to apply directly and clarify during the recruiter call whether your timezone works for the specific team you are joining.

How difficult is the Cursor interview process?

Based on publicly reported accounts, the bar is high but the process is designed to assess real capability, not trick you with obscure puzzles. The technical rounds reward strong fundamentals and genuine product thinking over rehearsed algorithm solutions. Candidates who have used Cursor extensively and can talk concretely about projects they have shipped tend to perform better than those who only prepare on competitive coding platforms.

Do I need a machine learning background to get a role at Cursor?

Not for every role. Cursor hires for full-stack engineering, product management, design, and go-to-market positions as well. That said, even for non-ML roles, a working understanding of how AI tools function is increasingly expected given the nature of the product. For core engineering roles that touch AI features directly, LLM knowledge is genuinely important.

What compensation does Cursor offer?

Cursor does not publish salary bands publicly. For US-based roles, data on sites like levels.fyi suggests compensation at AI-focused startups of this stage tends to be competitive with larger tech companies. For remote roles based in India, compensation structures vary by role and seniority and are best discussed directly with the recruiter early in the process.

How many open roles does Cursor have right now?

As of July 2026, Cursor had 119 open roles across engineering, ML, product, design, and business functions. The number changes as positions are filled and new ones open, so check their careers page regularly if you are actively looking.

What is the single best way to stand out as a Cursor applicant?

Use the product seriously before you apply and form real, specific opinions about it. Build a project that uses or extends AI coding tools and share it publicly with a short write-up. A specific, thoughtful note about your actual experience with Cursor goes much further than a generic application, because it shows you are already the kind of user they are building for.
